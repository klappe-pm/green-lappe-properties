Brief · Why a field guide

A brand that holds
together, even when
nobody's looking.

A field guide is not a logo file. It is the operating manual the brand uses to stay consistent across hundreds of small touchpoints — a monthly statement, a Zillow listing, a maintenance update text, a renewal letter, a yard sign. Read this once to understand the brand. Reach for it again whenever a new surface needs to be built.

What this gives you

One voice across every surface. The cold email and the maintenance push and the lease addendum all sound like the same person wrote them — because the same five principles run through all of them.

One palette, one type system, one set of components. Decisions made once, applied everywhere, no debate on the next email template.

Why it matters

Two audiences are scanning for tells. Small landlords burned by chains. Renters tired of Greystar units and Craigslist scams. Both walk in skeptical.

Consistency is the strongest signal that this is a small operation that takes the work seriously. Trust earned during shopping has to survive a full lease cycle. The system is what makes that possible.

Reach for Part Two when building a real surface — an email, a statement, a portal screen. Every decision is documented at the token level so the system can be reproduced without re-explaining it.

Part one · 01 · Orientation

How to read
this guide.

This document has two halves. Part One explains the concepts a design system is built from. Part Two documents the specific decisions made for Green Property Solutions. The two halves are written so any reader — and any automated tool — can apply the system without re-explaining it.

What the brand owes owners

A monthly statement that reads like a bank statement, not a sales pitch. A repair workflow that confirms an ETA before work begins. A lease renewal that arrives in plain language, not legalese.

The system documents every touchpoint so the experience is consistent across the statement, the email, and the portal screen.

What the brand owes renters

A listing with real photos, real facts, and a real person reading the application. A tour confirmed in writing, on time. A maintenance request acknowledged inside an hour.

The acquisition surface and the resident portal share the same voice and the same components, so trust earned during shopping does not collapse after move-in.

How automation keeps it consistent

Every color, font, weight, and component is encoded as a token. A new email template, a new push notification, a new vendor mockup all reach through the same token layer.

The brand can be reproduced without re-explaining it — by a designer, a contractor, or any tool wired to read this guide.

Part one · 02 · Voice

What is brand voice?

Voice is how the brand sounds, every time it speaks. The same rhythm runs from a cold email subject line to a maintenance push notification to the legal footer of a lease. When the cadence is steady, the brand feels like one person.

What it is

A set of rules for how the brand writes. Voice covers sentence length, vocabulary, what state to use ("renter" vs "resident"), what gets capitalized, what words are off the menu, and how warm or cool the language runs.

It is not slogans or taglines. It is the operating system of every sentence the brand emits.

A brand without a voice document drifts into whatever language the writer of the day prefers. Three people writing on three days produce three brands.

Why it matters here

Two skeptical audiences are scanning for tells that this is the same outfit they've been burned by before. Voice is the strongest tell, faster than logo or color.

If the listing copy and the statement footer sound like the same person wrote them, the brand reads as one operator. If they sound like a marketing department and a back office, the brand reads as a chain.

The five voice principles in §2 are the constraint that keeps everything sounding like Megan, even when Megan isn't the one typing.

For Green Property Solutions

The voice is direct, specific, accountable, local, calm. Short sentences. Numbers before adjectives. “Bothell,” never “the Puget Sound region.” The brand says renter before a lease, resident after. Words like boutique, concierge, and passionate never appear.

Part one · 03 · Color

What are
color tokens?

A token is a named color with a specific job. The brand has a small library of named tokens, organized in three layers, so that an email, a printed sign, and a portal screen always reach for the same paint can.

Layer 01 Primitives

Raw hex values with names. Cedar #2D6A4F. Cream #FBF6EC. Seven of them. This is the menu the system is allowed to cook with.

→
Layer 02 Semantic tokens

Primitives renamed by job. --color-brand points to Cedar. --color-action points to Clay. The job is what code references · not the hex.

→
Layer 03 Components

A button reads --color-action. A statement border reads --color-brand. Swap Clay for a new accent and every button shifts in lockstep.

Why this matters

If a button were styled with the hex value #A95C42 directly, that color would be impossible to change without hunting through hundreds of files. Tokens make brand evolution a one-line edit.

The rule

No code, no print file, no email references a hex value. Everything reaches through a token. The token layer is the only place the brand's colors are written down.

For Green Property Solutions

Seven primitives (Cedar, Ink, Cream, Paper, Stone, Clay, Sky), four derived neutrals for text hierarchy (ink-80 through ink-20), and four system colors for state (success, warning, error, info). Fifteen named tokens total. Production code reaches only through token names; no hex value is referenced directly. Adding a primitive requires governance review.

Part one · 04 · Typography

How type
carries voice.

Type is how the brand reads out loud. The system runs on three faces, each with one job: a contemporary grotesque sans for the work, a warm editorial serif for the words, and a single italic accent for the named operator's signature.

Geist · The work

A modern grotesque sans-serif that sets the emotional pitch of the brand in one beat: serious, regional, current. It does not look like the navy serif of legacy property managers and it does not look like a SaaS gradient startup.

Geist covers headlines, navigation, buttons, forms, statements, and every tabular figure. One face, four weights, tabular numerals enabled.

Newsreader · The words

A warm editorial serif for prose, long-form copy, owner letters, and any surface where the reader is expected to settle in and read.

Newsreader gives the brand a literary register without going corporate. It is what makes a monthly statement feel like a letter from a person, not a notice from a chain.

Fraunces · The name

One italic accent, used once per document, to sign the work. Fraunces appears in the signature pattern (“Megan” in italic cedar) and almost nowhere else.

A third face is justified because the brand's whole promise is named accountability. The italic flourish is that promise made visible.

For Green Property Solutions

Three faces, three jobs. Geist runs every headline, button, navigation link, statement number, and form label. Newsreader runs every paragraph, lede, owner letter, and blog post. Fraunces appears once per document, italic, in Cedar, to sign Megan at the close of a letter or proposal.

Part one · 05 · Audience modes

Same brand,
different rooms.

A mode is the brand wearing the right clothes for a context. One brand can speak to a renter scrolling Zillow on the bus and an owner reading a March statement at the kitchen table, without sounding like two companies.

What changes between modes

Surface. Cream for warmth and discovery. Paper for cool operational focus.

Density. A listing page has one photo and three facts. A statement has thirty line items.

Imagery. Renters need full-bleed property photos. Owners need a sample of an actual statement.

Headline pitch. "2BR in Ballard. $2,400. June 15." vs "March statement ready. $4,200 net."

What stays constant

The components. The button, the card, the input, the callout, the signature block. Same form everywhere.

The voice. Direct, specific, accountable, local, calm · in every mode.

The named operator. Megan signs the work in all four modes. The language native to each audience changes; the accountability does not.

Without modes, a single design fights itself on every page. With modes, the brand fits the moment and the moment never confuses what brand it's in.

For Green Property Solutions

Five modes, declared via the data-audience attribute: neutral-acquisition (the landing page), owner-acquisition (pricing, sample statements, proposal request), owner-product (authenticated owner portal), renter-acquisition (the listings and tour flow), renter-product (resident portal). Each has a documented surface color, density, imagery rule, and headline pitch.

Part one · 06 · Components

Reusable pieces,
not reinvented
sentences.

A component is a sentence the brand says, in a fixed grammar. The statement card says "here is what happened this month." The listing card says "here is a real place, at this price, available now." The signature block says "this came from me." Reuse the form; the content changes.

Why fixed components matter

Every time a brand reinvents a button or a card, it loses a small unit of recognizability. Twelve slightly different statement layouts read as twelve different companies.

Fixed components make a brand reliable. The owner who opens their tenth statement knows exactly where to look for the bottom-line number. The renter who opens their third listing knows where the tour button is.

What gets to be a component

Anything that appears more than twice across the system: buttons, statement cards, listing cards, form inputs, callouts, the signature block. Components live in §9 with their exact CSS and their usage rules.

One-off marketing layouts are not components. They are compositions built from components.

For Green Property Solutions

Six components carry most of the brand: the button (Clay primary, Cedar outlined secondary, quiet underlined tertiary); the statement card (Cedar left border on Paper); the listing card (full-bleed photo on Cream with one Clay CTA); the form input (Cedar focus ring, four states); the callout (five color-coded states, always paired with an icon and a label); the signature block (Fraunces italic “Megan” closing every letter).

§ 3.4System colors · what each one tells the user

Brand colors express identity. System colors carry information. Each maps to a user state, paired with an icon and a label so the meaning never relies on color alone.

--color-success

It worked.

Used for: Paid · Approved · Completed. Confirms an action finished cleanly · no follow-up required.

--color-warning

Heads up.

Used for: Pending · Due soon · Expiring. Needs attention before a deadline. Not yet urgent.

--color-error

Act now.

Used for: Failed · Overdue · Requires action. The action did not complete or has crossed a deadline.

--color-info

For your reference.

Used for: Note · Hint · Background context. Carries information without requiring action.

§ 11 · Photography direction

The brand has a face.
Use it.

Photography earns the brand promise of named accountability. Real PNW homes, including the imperfect parts. No stock.

Do · photograph

Don't · photograph

Handshakes. Suited-couple-receiving-keys.

Drone over Mt. Rainier. Seattle skyline at dusk.

Houses with sunbeams and perfect lawns.

The category is saturated with these. Refusing them is the brand. When illustration is used, it's hand-drawn ink texture over flat color blocks, brand palette only, real subject matter.

§ 14 · Quick reference

Five lines that
govern every
decision.

Rule 01

Clay = click.

Nowhere else. No decorative Clay, no Clay text in prose, no Clay backgrounds for non-action content.

Rule 02

Seven primitives. No more.

Cedar, Ink, Cream, Paper, Stone, Clay, Sky. Additions require governance review.

Rule 03

renter → resident.

Pre-customer is "renter." Authenticated tenant is "resident." Owner stays owner. Never "tenant portal."

Rule 04

Megan signs the work.

A named operator appears in every mode. Same person, same accountability, expressed in language native to each audience.

Rule 05 · governs

State colors are scoped outside the brand.

Brand colors express identity. System colors carry information. Never overload Cedar with "success" or Clay with "error." Every state surface pairs color with an icon and a text label.
