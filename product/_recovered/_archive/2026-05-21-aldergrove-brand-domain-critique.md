# Critique of the `green<function>pnw.com` Pattern

> [!success] Recommendation

Consolidate to one primary root (`greenpnw.com` or, stronger, `greenpmpnw.com`) with path-based owner/renter segmentation, hold 3 functional siblings as 301 redirects (`greenpropertymanagementpnw.com`, `greenrentalspnw.com`, `greenownerspnw.com`), and register `greenrealtypnw.com` defensively on a completely separate root that the PM business never touches; if Megan is serious about a future real estate brand, the stronger move is Alternative 2: pick a non-surname brand for the PM business entirely (something like `Lappe PM` or a geographic brand) and fully preserve "Green" for the sales business, because surname-as-PM-brand spends optionality that is hard to get back, and "Green" as a modifier is doing weaker brand work than the surname interpretation assumes.

## The Brand

### Name Candidates

Working from three naming families, each with different tradeoffs:

**Family A: Geographic-functional.** Names that anchor to PNW geography and signal the category directly. Easy to brief, easy to SEO, lower brand ceiling.

- `Cascade PM` / `Cascade Rentals` / `Cascade Property Co`
- `Puget PM` / `Puget Rental Co`
- `Sound Rentals` / `Sound Property Management`
- `Northshore PM`
- `Salish Property Co`

**Family B: Coined or evocative.** Names that do not describe the business but feel right for it. Higher brand ceiling, more work to build recognition, easier to firewall from the real estate arm.

- `Klappe` (Megan's partner's surname, if available; functions as a coined word to most ears)
- `Aldergrove` (PNW-native tree, geographic feel, not a real place in WA)
- `Foghouse` / `Fogline`
- `Tideline`
- `Headwater`
- `Coppice`

**Family C: Owner-promise.** Names that signal the owner-side value proposition (yield, fill rate, hands-off). Risk: too B2B, alienates the renter side of the marketplace.

- `Filled` / `Filled PM`
- `Leased`
- `Rentline`
- `Doorstead`-style (taken, but the pattern: `Doorpost`, `Doorframe`)

### Recommended Primary: `Aldergrove`

The argument:

- **Distinctive.** Not a real Washington place (there is an Aldergrove in BC, far enough away to not collide). No existing PNW property management company under this name as of a quick check; verify before committing.
- **PNW-native.** Red alder is the dominant deciduous tree in western Washington and Oregon. Locals will read it as regional without needing a "PNW" suffix in the domain. This eliminates the awkward `pnw.com` pattern.
- **Operational feel.** "Grove" connotes a managed, cultivated stand of trees. Trees that someone tends. The metaphor maps directly to a portfolio of properties under management.
- **Voice latitude.** The name is calm and infrastructural enough to support a serious PM voice, but soft enough to not feel corporate or cold to renters.
- **Firewall-clean.** Shares no phonetic, visual, or semantic territory with "Green Realty" or any plausible Megan Green sales brand. The two businesses can coexist without contamination in either direction.
- **Domain availability.** `aldergrove.com` is almost certainly taken; `aldergrovepm.com`, `aldergroverentals.com`, `aldergroveco.com`, `aldergrove.co` are likely available. Run the check.

Backup if `Aldergrove` is unavailable or collides: `Coppice` (a coppice is a managed grove harvested on rotation; same metaphor, more obscure word, harder to spell). Second backup: `Cascade PM` (geographic-functional, safer, lower ceiling).

The rest of this document assumes `Aldergrove` as the working brand. The strategy is portable to the backups.

## Positioning

**Category:** Residential property management for small-portfolio owners in King and Snohomish counties.

**Primary audience (paying side):** Owners of 1 to 10 rental units in King and Snohomish counties. Disproportionately rate-locked accidental landlords (the people who bought between 2018 and 2022 at sub-4% rates and cannot sell without losing the loan). Secondary: intentional small landlords with 2 to 10 doors who have outgrown self-management.

**Secondary audience (supply side):** Renters in King and Snohomish counties looking for well-maintained units with responsive management. They are not the customer, but they are the product experience, and a bad renter experience kills the owner pitch.

**Positioning statement:** Aldergrove is the property management company for small-portfolio owners in King and Snohomish counties who want their rentals run like infrastructure: quietly, reliably, and without drama.

**What this rejects:**

- The "tech-forward disruption" pitch (Doorstead, Belong, Mynd). Owners in this segment have been burned by churn at the venture-backed PMs and are skeptical of slick UX as a substitute for operational competence.
- The "we are landlords too" pitch (most local PMs). Overused, signals nothing.
- The "premium boutique" pitch (high-touch concierge). Small-portfolio owners are price-sensitive on the management fee and want competence, not white-glove.

**What this commits to:**

- Operational reliability over feature breadth.
- Communication discipline over communication volume.
- Renter experience as an owner-side value (lower turnover, lower vacancy, lower legal exposure).
- Local concentration over geographic expansion.

## Brand Voice

**Three voice attributes, in priority order:**

1. **Plainspoken.** Short sentences. Concrete nouns. No jargon, no buzzwords, no real-estate-agent cadence ("Welcome home!"). If a sentence could appear in a brochure for any other PM, rewrite it.
2. **Competent.** The voice of someone who has done this before, knows what goes wrong, and is not surprised. Calm under pressure. Specific where competitors are vague.
3. **Quietly warm.** Not cold, not corporate. The warmth comes from being treated like an adult, not from exclamation points or emoji.

**Voice rules:**

- Active voice. Almost always.
- No em dashes (per the operator's preference; this carries to the brand).
- No "we are passionate about." No "your trusted partner." No "peace of mind." These phrases are dead.
- Numbers over adjectives. "Average 11 days to lease" beats "fast leasing."
- Acknowledge tradeoffs. "Our fee is 8%, higher than the 6% bargain shops, because we do X, Y, Z" beats "competitive pricing."
- Address owners and renters in the voice each one needs. Owners: ROI, risk, time. Renters: clarity, speed, respect.

**Voice swatches:**

Owner-facing, homepage hero:

> Aldergrove manages rental homes in King and Snohomish counties for owners with one to ten doors. We answer the phone, we file the paperwork, and we keep the unit rented. The fee is 8% of collected rent. No setup fees, no markup on maintenance, no renewal fees. Ask us anything.

Renter-facing, listing page:

> 2 bed, 1 bath in Ballard. Available June 1. $2,450/month. Pets considered. The application is $45 and takes about 15 minutes. We respond to applications within one business day. Showings Tuesday and Saturday; book below.

Owner-facing, monthly statement email:

> Your March statement is attached. Rent collected on time. One maintenance request (kitchen faucet, $85, vendor invoice attached). Net to you: $2,165, deposited Friday. Lease renewal is due in August; we will reach out in June with renewal recommendation and market comp.

Renter-facing, maintenance acknowledgment:

> Got it. A plumber will be in touch within one business day to schedule. If it is an emergency (active leak, no heat, no hot water), call the number in your lease and someone will pick up.

**What the voice is not:**

- Not jokey. Not memey. Not "We get it, landlording is hard!"
- Not aspirational. Not "Building wealth, one door at a time."
- Not apologetic. Not "Sorry for the inconvenience!" when no inconvenience was caused.

## Visual Identity

The visual system supports the positioning: infrastructural, calm, competent, regionally PNW without being cliché. It deliberately rejects three visual languages that dominate the category:

1. **The Compass / Belong / Doorstead aesthetic:** clean sans-serif, oversized hero photography of attractive young homeowners, lots of white space, gradient buttons. Reads as venture-backed and disposable.
2. **The local-Realtor aesthetic:** stock photos of houses, gold or burgundy accents, Times New Roman, "Serving the Eastside since 1987." Reads as dated.
3. **The eco-greenwashed aesthetic:** sage green, leaf icons, kraft paper textures, "sustainable." This is the exact aesthetic to avoid, both because the brand is not green-the-color and because the eco read is the trap we are escaping by abandoning the "Green" surname.

### Color Palette

The palette is built around a deep forest green that reads as PNW and as competent (not eco), warm neutrals that ground it, and a single accent for action.

**Primary:**

- `Alder` (deep forest green): `#2D4A3E`. The brand's signature color. Dark enough to function as a near-black for typography and large surfaces. Reads PNW without reading "eco."

**Secondaries:**

- `Bark` (warm dark brown): `#3D2E24`. Used for body text on light backgrounds, secondary surfaces.
- `Lichen` (muted pale green): `#C8D1C4`. Used for backgrounds, cards, low-emphasis surfaces. The single concession to "greenness," used sparingly.
- `Fog` (warm off-white): `#F4F1EB`. Primary background. Not pure white; pure white reads as clinical and disposable.

**Accents:**

- `Ember` (burnt orange): `#C65D2E`. The single action color. Used for primary buttons, key links, and critical UI moments. Used scarcely; its scarcity is what gives it weight.
- `Slate` (cool gray-blue): `#5C6770`. Used for tertiary text, dividers, and disabled states.

**Why this palette:**

- The green is dark and earthy, not bright or grassy. It reads as Douglas fir and red alder, not as a sustainability claim.
- The neutrals are warm. Cool grays would push the brand toward tech-startup; warm browns and off-whites push it toward residential and lived-in.
- The accent is orange, not green or blue. Orange is uncommon in PM and real estate, where the defaults are blue (trust-bank cliché) or green (eco cliché). Orange distinguishes the brand on every CTA and chart.
- The palette holds up in print, in low-light digital, and in single-color applications (signage, embroidery, stamps).

### Typography

Two typefaces, one serif for display and one sans for body and UI. Both are free and open-source to keep the system buildable without licensing friction.

**Display: `Fraunces`** (Google Fonts). A modern serif with variable axes (soft/hard, slant). Used for headlines, marketing pages, signage. Set with slight optical adjustments: lower contrast, softer terminals. Conveys craft and care without feeling traditional or stuffy.

**Body and UI: `Inter`** (Google Fonts). A workhorse neutral sans. Used for body copy, forms, dashboards, statements, contracts. Maximally legible at small sizes, neutral enough to disappear.

**Type rules:**

- Headlines: Fraunces, weight 500, tight tracking, dark Alder or Bark.
- Subheads: Inter, weight 600, normal tracking.
- Body: Inter, weight 400, 1.5 line-height, Bark on Fog or Fog on Alder.
- Numbers: Inter tabular figures throughout. Owner statements and listings depend on numbers aligning vertically.
- Never set Fraunces below 18px. It is a display face.
- Never set Inter above 32px. It is a body face.

### Logo and Mark

The logo system is a wordmark plus an optional symbol. The wordmark is the primary expression; the symbol is for app icons, social avatars, and signage.

**Wordmark:** `aldergrove` set in Fraunces 500, all lowercase, slight letter-spacing reduction. Lowercase because uppercase reads as institutional and the brand is deliberately not institutional. Lowercase Fraunces has personality without being twee.

**Symbol:** A geometric mark derived from an alder leaf or an alder catkin (the slender, drooping flower clusters that hang from alder branches in late winter). The catkin is the stronger choice: it is distinctive, very few brands use it, and the vertical drooping line gives the mark a calm, gravitational feeling. Rendered as a single line drawing in Alder green.

**Lockup:** Wordmark and symbol can lock up horizontally (symbol left, wordmark right) or stack (symbol above, wordmark below). The wordmark alone should carry 80% of brand surface area; the symbol alone should appear in 15%; the lockup in 5%.

### Pattern and Texture

A single brand pattern, used sparingly. The pattern is a vertical line motif derived from the alder catkin: thin vertical strokes of varying lengths, arranged in irregular columns. Used as:

- Subtle background texture on email headers and section dividers.
- Print pattern on owner welcome packets and renter move-in materials.
- Foil-stamped or debossed on physical mail (yes, physical mail; see content strategy below).

The pattern is never used as a screen-fill background for body content. Its job is to mark transitions and territory, not to decorate.

### Photography

Photography is the highest-leverage and most-abused element in PM branding. The defaults to reject:

- Stock photos of houses with green lawns and blue skies. (Generic, dated, lying about PNW weather.)
- Stock photos of smiling couples with keys. (Insulting to renters, condescending to owners.)
- Drone shots of suburban developments. (Wrong audience; this is small-portfolio, not HOA management.)

The brand's photography direction:

- **Real units, real conditions, real light.** Listings photographed in actual PNW light (often overcast, often interior-lit). No HDR, no virtual staging, no wide-angle distortion. The honesty is the brand.
- **Detail over hero.** Close shots of door hardware, kitchen tile, the corner of a window with rain on it. The brand earns trust by showing it pays attention to details.
- **People rarely, and only as documentation.** No models. If a person appears, it is a real Aldergrove team member doing real work, photographed plainly.
- **PNW environmental shots, used sparingly.** Fog in alder trees, wet pavement, a moss-covered fence. Used in long-form content and brand pages, never as decoration on transactional surfaces.

### Iconography

Icons are line-only, single-weight (1.5px at 24px), rounded terminals, Alder green. Use the `Lucide` icon set as the base; customize only when a needed icon is missing. Do not mix icon styles.

## Content Strategy

The brand publishes deliberately and infrequently. The publication calendar is not a content marketing engine; it is a trust-building artifact.

**Three content pillars:**

1. **Market data.** Quarterly rental market reports for King and Snohomish counties: vacancy rates, rent comps by neighborhood, leasing velocity, days on market. Published as a downloadable PDF and a web page. Owners use these to benchmark their units. The reports are the single best lead-generation asset a small PM can produce, and almost no one does them well.
2. **Operational transparency.** Quarterly "how we did" posts: average days to lease, average maintenance response time, average tenant tenure, fee transparency. Numbers, not narrative. This is unusual in the category and earns disproportionate trust.
3. **Owner education.** Long-form, evergreen pieces on the actual operational questions small landlords have: when to renew vs. re-list, how Seattle's just-cause eviction ordinance actually works, how to handle a tenant who stops paying, when an LLC actually matters. These are not blog posts; they are reference documents.

**What the brand does not publish:**

- "5 Tips for New Landlords" listicles.
- Holiday posts. (No Memorial Day content.)
- Anything on Instagram beyond listings and the occasional team photo.
- Anything on TikTok.

**Channels, in priority order:**

1. The owner-facing email list. Quarterly market reports plus one monthly operations note. This is the brand's most important channel.
2. The website's owner section. Updated quarterly with the latest report and educational pieces.
3. Physical mail to a target list of King and Snohomish small landlords. Twice a year. Real paper, real signature, real return address. This is the move that actual competitors are not making.
4. Google Business Profile and local SEO. Reviews from owners, not renters.
5. Instagram, minimally. Listings and occasional team photos. Not a marketing channel; a credibility check.

## Style Guide Rules (the short version)

- **Logo clear space:** equal to the height of the lowercase `a` in the wordmark, on all sides.
- **Minimum logo size:** 80px wide digital, 0.75" wide print.
- **Logo never:** stretched, recolored outside the approved palette, set on a busy photographic background without a solid backing, set in any typeface other than Fraunces.
- **Color contrast:** all body text must meet WCAG AA at minimum. Bark on Fog and Fog on Alder both pass. Do not set body text on Lichen (insufficient contrast).
- **Photography crop:** always rectangular, never circular, never with shadows. Rounded corners (8px) on web, hard corners in print.
- **Voice in subject lines:** no exclamation points, no all-caps, no "URGENT," no emoji. Subject lines describe the content.
- **Numbers:** always tabular, always with explicit units (`$2,450/mo` not `$2,450`).
- **Dates:** always written out (`June 1, 2026`) in customer-facing copy; ISO format (`2026-06-01`) in internal/operational copy.

## What to Debate

The choices most worth challenging:

1. **The brand name itself.** `Aldergrove` is a specific bet on an evocative coined-feeling name over a geographic-functional name. The safe alternative is `Cascade PM` or similar: easier to brief, easier to SEO, lower brand ceiling. The case for `Aldergrove` rests on the assumption that the brand will be built over 5+ years and benefits from a higher ceiling. If the horizon is shorter or the marketing budget is thinner, `Cascade PM` is the more conservative call.
2. **The orange accent.** `Ember` orange is a deliberate distinguishing choice, but it is also a risk. Orange polarizes; blue is safe. The argument for orange is that every other PM in the category uses blue or green, and a single distinguishing accent compounds over time. The argument against is that owners may read orange as "alarming" on a financial statement. Test it on actual owner statements before committing.
3. **The renter-as-product-experience framing.** The strategy explicitly treats renters as the product, not the customer. This is operationally correct but ethically uncomfortable to some operators. An alternative framing treats renters as a co-equal audience with their own brand promise. The case for the current framing is honesty about who pays; the case against is that renters who feel like products tend to leave bad reviews.
4. **The physical mail channel.** Real paper to a targeted list is expensive per touch and slow to test. The case for it is that no one in the category is doing it, so the inbox is empty and signal-to-noise is extreme. The case against is that it is a 12-to-18 month investment before you know if it works.
5. **`Fraunces` as display face.** Fraunces is a fashionable choice in 2024 to 2026, which means it will look dated by 2030. A more conservative serif (`Source Serif`, `Lora`) will age better. The case for Fraunces is that the brand needs personality now to break through; the case against is that personality is a depreciating asset.

The recommendation stands: build Aldergrove as a calm, infrastructural, regionally-PNW property management brand with deep green and warm neutrals, plainspoken voice, infrequent and substantive content, and a deliberate firewall from any future Megan Green real estate work. Each of the five debate points above is a real tradeoff worth pressure-testing before committing.

## Format

- Direct prose, no opening filler.
- Mermaid diagram for the architecture visualization.
- End with a one-paragraph recommendation.

The pattern is structurally weak as a brand system. It works as a defensive SEO and redirect net, not as identity architecture. Three problems compound:

**Equity fragmentation.** Five sibling domains at the root level give users five plausible "front doors" with no signal which is canonical. Owners typing `greenpropertiespnw.com` and renters typing `greenrentalspnw.com` land in different SEO contexts, build separate backlink profiles, and split brand recall. Sibling domains do not pass authority to each other the way subdomains or paths do under a single root. Running five roots means running five SEO programs or, more realistically, one program with four parasitic dead weights.

**"Green" is ambiguous.** As a surname it is invisible; as a modifier it reads as eco/sustainability. `greenhomespnw.com` and `greenpropertiespnw.com` will be parsed by most users and search engines as "environmentally responsible homes in the PNW," not "Megan Green's homes." This is not fatal, but it means the surname-as-brand strategy is doing less work than intended. The brand has to earn the surname interpretation through visual identity and copy, every time.

**PNW competitive collision.** Existing operators using "Green" in PNW real estate include Green Group at Windermere, GreenWorks Realty (defunct but indexed), and several "Greenhouse" branded sales and PM shops in Seattle and Portland. A surname-based brand will fight for SERP real estate against these and against the eco-real-estate category. This is survivable but not a moat.

**Domain load-bearing assessment:**

|Domain|Role|
|---|---|
|`greenpropertymanagementpnw.com`|SEO workhorse for "property management" queries, redirect to primary|
|`greenpropertiespnw.com`|Weak primary candidate, generic, ambiguous|
|`greenhomespnw.com`|Renter-facing or sales-facing, conflicts with future real estate arm|
|`greenrentalspnw.com`|Renter-side SEO catch, redirect|
|`greenownerspnw.com`|Owner-side SEO catch, redirect or owner-only landing|

None of these are strong primaries. `greenpropertymanagementpnw.com` is the most descriptively accurate but the longest and least brandable. `greenhomespnw.com` is the shortest but actively burns optionality for Megan's future sales business, which violates a stated constraint.

## The Debate

**Case for multi-domain segmentation.** Two-sided marketplaces have genuinely different audiences with different jobs-to-be-done. Owners want yield, vacancy data, and trust signals. Renters want listings, application speed, and responsiveness. Separate domains let each audience land in a context built for them, with separate analytics, separate ad accounts, and no cross-contamination of messaging. Separate domains also let the rental/PM brand and a future real estate brand live on entirely different roots, which is cleaner legally and operationally if Megan ever sells one and keeps the other.

**Case for consolidation.** A single root domain with subdomains (`owners.x.com`, `rent.x.com`) or paths (`x.com/owners`, `x.com/rent`) gives one canonical brand, one SEO program, one authority graph, and one trust anchor. Google treats subdomains as related entities and paths as fully unified. Audience segmentation is solved in UX, not DNS. The "two front doors" pattern is a well-established marketplace convention (Airbnb has `airbnb.com/host`, not `hosts.airbnb.com` as a separate brand). Multi-domain segmentation solves a problem most marketplaces do not actually have, while creating SEO, trust, and operational problems they definitely do have.

**Position: consolidate.** Run one primary domain with path-based segmentation for owners and renters. Hold 3 to 5 functional sibling domains as defensive redirects to the primary, not as parallel front doors. The two-sided audience problem is a UX problem. Solving it at the DNS level is over-engineering that creates real costs (SEO dilution, brand confusion, operational overhead) to solve a problem that path-based routing handles cleanly.

## Marketplace Design Implication

Domain architecture should be invisible to audience segmentation. Owners and renters should never need to know they are on the same root. What they need is:

- An unambiguous landing experience on first visit (clear "I am an owner" vs "I am a renter" entry).
- Separate navigation, copy, and visual treatment after that fork.
- Shared trust signals (one company, one license, one reputation).

Path-based routing (`x.com/owners`, `x.com/renters`) delivers all three. Separate domains deliver the first two but actively undermine the third, because owners doing due diligence on the company find a different domain with a different backlink profile and may not connect them. Trust compounds at the root; splitting roots splits trust.

## Alternatives

**Alternative 1: Consolidated single domain (recommended).**

- Primary: `greenpnw.com` or `greenpmpnw.com`.
- Architecture: `/owners` and `/rent` paths.
- Defensive redirects: 3 to 5 functional siblings (`greenpropertymanagementpnw.com`, `greenrentalspnw.com`, `greenownerspnw.com`).
- Future real estate arm: separate root (`greenrealtypnw.com` or `megangreen.com`), held but not developed.
- Pros: unified SEO, unified trust, clean firewall to future real estate brand, cheap to operate.
- Cons: requires disciplined UX fork on landing; less SEO surface for exact-match functional queries.

**Alternative 2: Non-surname brand, preserve Green for real estate.**

This is the stronger long-term move if Megan is serious about a future sales business. Use a non-surname brand for the PM business so "Green" is fully preserved.

- Primary brand candidates: `Lappe PM`, `Cascade Rent`, `Sound Rentals`, `Pacific Lease`, `Northshore PM`, `Lappe & Co`, or a coined name.
- Primary domain: `[brand]pnw.com` or `[brand].com`.
- Defensive functional redirects: same pattern (`[brand]rentals.com`, `[brand]owners.com`).
- Future Megan Green real estate: completely independent brand, completely independent domain, no shared assets.
- Pros: zero contamination of "Green" by rental category; PM brand can be acquired or sold without touching Megan's name; "Green Realty" or equivalent is fully unburned.
- Cons: gives up the surname-as-personal-trust signal on the PM side; requires building a new brand from zero.

**Alternative 3: Geographic anchor as primary, surname as endorsement.**

- Primary: `pugetsoundpm.com` or `cascadepm.com`.
- Tagline / endorsement: "A Megan Green company" or "Founded by Megan Green."
- Future real estate arm: `greenrealty.com` or similar, fully separate.
- Pros: geographic anchor is sharper than "PNW" (which spans Oregon, Washington, BC, Idaho), and the surname operates as a trust endorsement, not a brand prefix. Easy to firewall.
- Cons: requires building two brands eventually.
