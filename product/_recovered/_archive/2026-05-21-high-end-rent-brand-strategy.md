# What Changes When the Rent Is $4,000 to $6,000

The shift from mid-market ($2,000 to $3,500) to high-end ($4,000 to $8,000+) is not a quantitative change in the same business. It is a qualitatively different business with a different customer, different unit economics, different operational requirements, and a different brand. The earlier recommendation has to be re-examined from the ground up, not adjusted at the margins.

## The Owner Changes

The mid-market owner is dominantly a rate-locked accidental landlord: 1 to 3 doors, bought the unit to live in, kept it as a rental when life moved them elsewhere, cost-sensitive on the management fee, anxious about vacancy. The high-end owner is structurally different:

- **Intentional investor or relocated executive.** They either bought the property as an investment with eyes open, or they own a $1.5M to $3M home they relocated out of (often to the Bay Area, New York, or internationally for work) and are renting until they return or decide to sell.
- **Less fee-sensitive, more risk-sensitive.** The 2-point difference between an 8% and a 10% fee on a $6,000/mo unit is $1,440/year. The risk of a bad tenant in a $2M home is six figures. They will pay the higher fee for actual underwriting discipline.
- **Often remote.** Disproportionately not in Washington. They need a manager who can be the eyes, ears, and signature on the ground. Communication discipline matters more than feature breadth.
- **Sophisticated about real estate.** Many own multiple properties. They have opinions about cap rates, depreciation, 1031s, and will ask questions a mid-market owner does not. The brand has to meet them at that level without being condescending.
- **Reputation-sensitive.** The house is often their former primary residence or a meaningful asset. They care how it is presented, who lives in it, and how it is maintained in ways a pure-yield investor does not.

## The Renter Changes

The mid-market renter is rent-burdened, application-anxious, and shopping on price and availability. The high-end renter at $4,000 to $8,000/mo is a different profile:

- **Relocating professional, often corporate-funded.** Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Google, biotech, finance. Often on a 1 to 3 year assignment. Often has a relocation budget that covers the first month and deposit.
- **Comparing against buying.** At $6,000/mo, the renter is consciously choosing not to buy. They have done the math. They are renting because of optionality, not affordability.
- **High expectations on responsiveness.** They are accustomed to white-glove service in other parts of their life (corporate IT, executive assistant, concierge medicine). A 24-hour response time on a leaking dishwasher is unacceptable.
- **Sophisticated about leases.** They will read the lease, they will negotiate terms, they will ask about furnished options, early termination, pet policies, and home office allowances.
- **Quietly powerful.** A bad experience does not produce a Yelp review; it produces a phone call to a friend who is also renting at this tier, and that friend never enters the funnel.

## The Unit Changes

- $4,000 to $8,000/mo in King County means: single-family homes in Bellevue, Mercer Island, Kirkland, Sammamish, Madison Park, Magnolia, Queen Anne, Capitol Hill (large units), Laurelhurst, Wedgwood, Madrona, Ballard (premium new construction); luxury condos in downtown Seattle, Bellevue, and Kirkland. In Snohomish, it means waterfront in Edmonds, Mukilteo, parts of Mill Creek, and high-end Mill Creek/Bothell new construction.
- These units have features that change the operational picture: smart home systems, high-end appliances that break in expensive ways, landscaping that requires a service, sometimes pools or docks, sometimes wine cellars, sometimes home theaters. The maintenance vendor list is different. The cost of a wrong vendor is much higher.
- Turnover costs more in absolute dollars. A 30-day vacancy on a $6,000 unit is $6,000. Make-ready costs on a high-end unit can run $5,000 to $15,000.
- The lease is more complex. Furnished vs. unfurnished. Corporate vs. individual lessee. Pet addenda for actual breeds and not just deposits. Sometimes a clause about the owner's personal property remaining in the home.

## The Unit Economics Change

This is the part most operators miss when they "go upmarket." The numbers are different in ways that change the strategy.

|Metric|Mid-market ($2,500/mo)|High-end ($6,000/mo)|
|---|---|---|
|Management fee at 8%|$200/mo|$480/mo|
|Management fee at 10%|$250/mo|$600/mo|
|Annual revenue per door|$2,400 to $3,000|$5,760 to $7,200|
|Doors needed for $1M revenue|333 to 417|139 to 174|
|Vacancy cost (30 days)|$2,500|$6,000|
|Typical make-ready|$1,500 to $3,000|$5,000 to $15,000|
|Tenant tenure (typical)|14 to 18 months|18 to 36 months|
|Leasing-fee revenue per turn|$2,500 (one month)|$6,000 (one month)|

Implications:

- **Fewer doors, more revenue per door.** A 150-door high-end portfolio is roughly equivalent in revenue to a 400-door mid-market portfolio, with materially less operational surface area. This is a more sustainable small business.
- **The leasing fee matters more.** At $6,000/mo, a one-month leasing fee is $6,000 of revenue per turn. With longer tenure, leasing fees are less frequent but materially larger.
- **The cost of a bad tenant is asymmetric.** A bad tenant in a $6,000 unit can produce $30,000+ in damages, $40,000+ in unpaid rent during eviction, and a legal exposure that mid-market never sees. Underwriting tenants is the highest-leverage operational discipline.
- **Concentration risk is real.** 150 doors at $6,000/mo is $900,000/mo in rent flowing through the business. Losing 5 owners is meaningful in a way it is not at 400 doors.

## What This Does to the Brand Strategy

The core direction holds: non-surname brand, firewall to Megan's future real estate work, infrastructural positioning, plainspoken voice, deep green and warm neutrals. But the dials move significantly.

## Position Has to Shift up

`Aldergrove` as positioned in the prior document was calibrated for the mid-market: "rentals run like infrastructure, quietly and without drama." That is correct for mid-market, but for the high-end owner it under-promises in the wrong direction. The high-end owner does not just want infrastructure; they want a manager who treats the home with the care they would treat it themselves, and who treats the tenant well enough that the tenant treats the home well.

## The Revised Positioning

> Aldergrove manages high-end rental homes in King and Snohomish counties for owners who want their property treated like a home, not a yield. We underwrite tenants seriously, we communicate when there is something to say, and we are the person on the ground when you are not.

## Differences from the Mid-market Positioning

- "Treated like a home, not a yield" reframes the brand around stewardship, which is what relocated owners actually want.
- "Underwrite tenants seriously" is the explicit promise that distinguishes high-end PM from mid-market PM. It is the single most important operational difference in the business.
- "Communicate when there is something to say" is a deliberate inversion of the high-touch-PM pitch. High-end owners hate noise as much as mid-market owners do; they want competence, not constant updates.
- "Person on the ground" addresses the relocated-owner pain point directly.

The brand is still calm and infrastructural; it is now also stewardship-oriented and explicitly underwriting-disciplined.

## Voice Tightens, Does Not Soften

The temptation is to make a luxury brand sound luxurious. This is wrong. The voice should get more precise, more numerate, and slightly more formal, but not more decorated. High-end owners distrust marketing voice; they trust competence voice.

Voice attribute changes:

- **Plainspoken → Precise.** Still no jargon, but more specificity. "Average 11 days to lease" becomes "Median 9 days to lease across 47 high-end placements in the last 24 months, with two outliers explained in our quarterly report."
- **Competent → Underwriting-disciplined.** The voice signals risk awareness without alarm. "We decline roughly 1 in 4 applicants. Our criteria are documented and we will tell you why."
- **Quietly warm → Quietly attentive.** The warmth becomes attentiveness to the property and the tenant, not friendliness to the owner.

## Voice Swatches at the High-end Tier

## Owner-facing, Homepage Hero

> Aldergrove manages high-end rental homes in King and Snohomish counties. Our average managed property rents for $5,800 a month. We have a 96% on-time rent collection rate and a median tenant tenure of 28 months. Our fee is 9% of collected rent plus one month at lease signing. Ask for our underwriting criteria; we will send them.

## Owner-facing, Monthly Statement Email

> March statement attached. Rent collected March 1, deposited March 3. One service call (irrigation timer replacement, $340, scheduled during the regular landscaping visit to avoid a separate trip). The tenant requested a quote for replacing the water heater proactively; the current unit is 11 years old. We recommend replacement before failure. Estimated $2,800 to $3,400; we will get three bids if you authorize.

## Renter-facing, Application Response

> Your application has been received. We review applications in the order received and typically respond within one business day. We will run credit, verify employment and income, contact two of your three listed references, and review your rental history. If we have questions, we will call you. If we approve, we will send the lease and move-in instructions. If we decline, we will tell you why.

The voice is doing more work per sentence. It is signaling competence through specificity, not through tone.

## Visual Identity: Refinement, Not Luxury

The luxury PM aesthetic in 2026 has converged on a small set of tropes: serif logos, sage and bone palettes, black-and-white photography of empty rooms, hairline rules, lots of negative space. This aesthetic is correct for the category and wrong for the brand, because it is what everyone else is doing.

The Aldergrove visual system shifts up in execution quality without joining the convention:

**Color palette adjustments:**

- `Alder` (deep forest green) `#2D4A3E`: stays as primary. Reads as more sophisticated at the high-end tier than at the mid-market tier, because the depth and warmth distinguish it from the cool sage that every luxury PM uses.
- `Bark` (warm dark brown) `#3D2E24`: stays.
- `Lichen` (muted pale green) `#C8D1C4`: removed. It was the eco-adjacent concession in the mid-market system; at the high-end tier it pulls toward the sage-cliché territory the brand needs to avoid.
- Replacement: `Bone` (warm cream) `#EDE6D3`. A richer off-white that reads as paper, plaster, and high-end interior surfaces. Holds better against `Alder` than the previous `Fog`.
- `Fog` (warm off-white) `#F4F1EB`: stays as secondary background.
- `Ember` (burnt orange) `#C65D2E`: stays as accent, used even more sparingly. At the high-end tier, the orange becomes a signature mark rather than a frequent CTA color. Most CTAs default to `Alder` on `Bone`; `Ember` appears once per page maximum.
- New addition: `Brass` (deep antique gold) `#9C7C38`. Used in print only, for foil-stamping on owner welcome packets, business cards, and physical mail. Never on screen. Brass is the move that signals high-end without going to the sage-and-marble trope; it reads as residential hardware, not hotel lobby.

**Typography adjustments:**

- **Display: `Fraunces` stays, but tightens.** Headlines set at a higher optical size (24px and up), with the soft axis dialed slightly harder for more authority. The mid-market system used Fraunces 500; the high-end system uses Fraunces 400 with tighter tracking.
- **Body: `Inter` is replaced by `Söhne` or `Neue Haas Grotesk` in branded marketing surfaces.** Inter is the right call for product UI (forms, dashboards, statements) because it is built for screens and free. But on the marketing site, owner welcome packets, and print materials, a licensed grotesque like Söhne signals craft in a way Inter cannot. Addition: Söhne, used because the high-end tier needs print-quality typography that Inter cannot provide at scale.
- **Numbers: tabular figures with old-style fallback for prose.** Owner statements use tabular lining figures. Marketing copy uses old-style figures for an editorial feel. This is a small detail that high-end owners notice.

**Photography direction shifts:**

- Mid-market photography: "real units, real conditions, real light." Hold that direction, but raise the production quality. A high-end listing photographed in honest PNW overcast light still needs a photographer who can light interiors, not someone with an iPhone.
- Add: architectural detail photography. Door hardware, kitchen counter edges, window mullions, stone fireplace surrounds, the patina on a maple floor. These shots become the brand's visual signature. They are not in the listings; they are on the marketing pages, in the welcome packets, and in the quarterly market reports.
- Add: tenant placement photography (with consent, rare). A photograph of a tenant's plants in the bay window of an Aldergrove-managed Madrona home, six months after move-in. The brand promise is that tenants live well in these homes; occasional documentary evidence is more credible than testimonials.
- Never: drone shots, twilight shots, virtually staged shots, real estate hero shots with grass and sky. These are sales photography; rental photography should look different.

**Logo and mark adjustments:**

- The wordmark in lowercase Fraunces 500 holds for the mid-market positioning; at the high-end tier, the wordmark should be set in **Fraunces 400 with slightly more letter-spacing**, which reads as more confident and less crafty. The lowercase choice stays; the brand is still not institutional.
- The catkin symbol holds, but at the high-end tier it appears with a foil-stamp treatment in `Brass` on physical materials. This is the single most distinguishing brand asset.
- The lockup proportion shifts: at the high-end tier, the wordmark and symbol are roughly equal weight on physical materials (business cards, welcome packets, signage), where they previously were 80/20 wordmark-dominant.

**Pattern adjustments:**

- The catkin pattern stays, but its application changes. At the mid-market tier, the pattern was used on email headers and section dividers. At the high-end tier, the pattern moves primarily to print and physical materials: endpaper on welcome packets, embossed on portfolio folders, debossed on business cards. On screen it appears only as a watermark on PDFs (market reports, monthly statements).

## Content Strategy: Lower Volume, Much Higher Quality

The mid-market content strategy was infrequent and substantive. At the high-end tier it becomes even less frequent and significantly more substantive.

The three pillars shift:

1. **Market data → high-end market data, with named submarkets.** The quarterly report at the high-end tier covers Bellevue, Mercer Island, Madison Park/Madrona, Queen Anne, Kirkland, Sammamish, Mukilteo waterfront, and Edmonds bowl. It reports vacancy at the submarket level, leasing velocity, days on market, and rent per square foot for units above $4,000/mo. No one publishes this data because the data set is small enough that submarket-level analysis requires the operator's own portfolio plus relationships with other high-end PMs to triangulate. This is the most valuable single asset the brand can produce, because it does not exist anywhere else.
2. **Operational transparency → underwriting transparency.** Publish the underwriting criteria. Publish the decline rate. Publish the median tenant tenure by submarket. High-end owners are obsessed with tenant quality, and no PM publishes their underwriting standards. Aldergrove publishes them. This is the single highest-trust-building move in the strategy.
3. **Owner education → property-specific stewardship guides.** Long-form pieces on the operational questions specific to high-end properties: when to replace vs. repair a tankless water heater, how to think about preventive maintenance on a slate roof, what an owner should know before renting to a corporate lessee on a 12-month relocation, how to handle a tenant request to install an EV charger in a 1925 Madison Park home with original electrical service. These are not blog posts; they are reference documents owners save and forward to other owners.

New addition at the high-end tier:

4. **The annual portfolio review.** Every owner gets a once-a-year written review of their property: rent positioning vs. submarket, capital improvement recommendations with ROI estimates, tenant satisfaction notes if applicable, recommendations for the next 12 months. This is the single deliverable that justifies the higher management fee. No mid-market PM produces this. Most high-end PMs do not produce this in writing.

**Channels shift:**

- Email list: stays as primary channel, but the audience is smaller and more targeted. The quarterly market report becomes a sought-after artifact; growth comes from owners forwarding it to other owners.
- Physical mail: amplified. The mid-market strategy used physical mail twice a year; the high-end strategy uses it quarterly, with the printed market report as the centerpiece. The mail is the brand at its highest production value.
- Website owner section: stays, but the published artifacts are higher-stakes (underwriting criteria, annual review samples, named-submarket data).
- Local PR: new channel. The quarterly market report is good enough to be quoted by the Seattle Times, GeekWire (for the relocation angle), and Puget Sound Business Journal. Building a relationship with two or three local reporters is high-leverage at this tier.
- Referrals from high-end real estate agents: critical channel. Many relocated owners come from agents who sold them their replacement home. Agent referral relationships at this tier require a different kind of business development than mid-market does.
- Instagram: stays minimal. Listings only, photographed at the production quality the brand demands. No team posts, no holiday posts.

## Operations and Pricing Implications

The brand has to be built on top of an operation that can actually deliver. At the high-end tier:

- **Fee structure.** 9% to 10% of collected rent, plus one month at lease signing. Premium markup is justified by underwriting discipline, communication quality, and the annual portfolio review. Setup fee waived in exchange for a 24-month minimum commitment. Maintenance is pass-through at vendor cost; no markup. The "no maintenance markup" commitment is itself a brand differentiator; many competitors quietly mark up 10% to 20%.
- **Underwriting.** Documented, published, defended. Minimum credit, income ratio (3x stricter at this tier because total dollar exposure is higher), employment verification, prior landlord references (not just the most recent), criminal background per WA law. Decline rate published quarterly.
- **Vendor list.** Curated, vetted, paid on time. High-end properties require vendors who can work in occupied homes without making a mess and without scratching a hardwood floor. The vendor relationships are an operational moat.
- **Communication SLAs.** Owner messages: response within 4 business hours. Tenant maintenance requests: acknowledged within 2 hours, vendor scheduled within 24 hours for non-emergencies, vendor on-site within 4 hours for emergencies. SLAs are published. Misses are reported in the monthly statement.
- **Insurance.** Errors and omissions coverage at $2M minimum, published. General liability at $2M minimum, published. Many mid-market PMs carry less; high-end owners will ask.
- **Portfolio cap.** The business explicitly caps doors per portfolio manager at 60 to 75 (vs. 100 to 150 at mid-market). This is the operational commitment that makes the SLAs achievable.

## What Does Not Change

- Non-surname brand. The argument for firewalling "Green" from PM holds equally at the high-end tier and arguably more strongly. A high-end PM business is more saleable as a standalone asset and benefits from being clearly separable from Megan's name.
- The primary green color, the warm neutrals, the orange accent (used more sparingly), the lowercase Fraunces wordmark, the catkin symbol.
- The plainspoken voice (now more precise, but still plain).
- The rejection of jokey, aspirational, or apologetic voice.
- The commitment to infrequent, substantive content over high-volume content marketing.
- The geographic concentration in King and Snohomish counties.

## What This Means for Go-to-market

Three structural choices about the business itself that follow from the high-end positioning:

1. **Start at the high end, do not graduate into it.** It is materially easier to start an Aldergrove-style PM at the high end and decline to take mid-market business than to start mid-market and try to move upmarket. Brand contamination flows one direction. If the first 20 doors are $6,000/mo units, the brand is high-end; if the first 20 doors are $2,500/mo units, every $6,000 owner who looks at the existing portfolio will read the brand as a mid-market PM stretching upmarket, which is what most local PMs are.
2. **The first 10 doors define the brand.** At the high-end tier, every property is a brand asset and every tenant is a brand ambassador. The first 10 placements should be in the named submarkets (Madison Park, Madrona, Bellevue, Mercer Island, Kirkland) and should be priced and presented in a way that establishes the standard. Taking a $3,500/mo unit early because the cash is helpful is a brand mistake that compounds.
3. **Agent relationships are the primary acquisition channel for the first 50 doors.** High-end relocation flows through real estate agents. The brand has to be sellable to agents (who refer their relocated clients) and through agents (who introduce Aldergrove to homeowners they have sold to). This means the brand needs to look credible to a Windermere Madison Park agent or a Compass Bellevue agent before it needs to look credible to a renter. There is a real tension here with Megan's potential future real estate work; the firewall has to be operational, not just visual. Aldergrove should not be referring sales business to Megan, and Megan should not be steering her sales clients to Aldergrove as a quid pro quo. The relationship should be that Aldergrove is good enough that any agent refers to it because it makes the agent look good, including (eventually) Megan, but without preferential treatment.

## The Debate at This Tier

The choices most worth pressure-testing for the high-end positioning:

1. **Whether to start high-end at all.** The high-end PM market in King County is smaller (perhaps 8,000 to 12,000 units in the $4,000+ range, of which a fraction are professionally managed) and more competitive at the top (firms like Real Property Management Eclipse, Windermere Property Management, and a handful of high-end-only boutiques). The case for starting here is that the unit economics are more sustainable and the brand has a clearer position. The case against is that the mid-market is much larger, lower-competition, and faster to fill the first 50 doors.
2. **Whether the brand can carry both tiers.** The current proposal builds a single brand calibrated for the high end. An alternative is to build two brands: `Aldergrove` for high-end and a separate brand (`Cascade PM` or similar) for mid-market. Operationally this is heavier. Strategically it preserves the high-end brand from contamination. At the scale of a starting business, one brand is correct; the question is whether to commit to one tier or position the brand to be tier-neutral.
3. **The 9% to 10% fee.** Pricing at 9% to 10% positions the brand as premium in a market where most PMs cluster at 8% to 9%. The case for premium pricing is that it filters for the right owners and funds the operational commitments. The case against is that fee comparison is the first thing prospective owners do, and pricing 100 to 200 basis points above market requires the brand to do real work to justify on every prospect call.
4. **The published underwriting criteria.** Publishing the criteria is a brand differentiator and a legal exposure. Fair housing requirements in Washington are strict, and published criteria become both a marketing asset and an obligation. The criteria have to be defensible and applied consistently. The case for publishing is that it is the single most credible high-trust signal; the case against is that it adds compliance overhead and exposure.
5. **Brass foil and physical mail at scale.** The premium production values (foil stamping, quarterly physical mail, printed market reports) are differentiators but they are expensive per touch. At 150 doors the cost is meaningful but bounded; at 50 doors it is a meaningful percentage of revenue. The case for is that the production values are the brand at the high-end tier; the case against is that they are a cost the brand cannot fully afford until it has scale, and there may be a phased rollout where the production values arrive after the first 30 doors.

The recommendation stands and strengthens at the high-end tier: build Aldergrove as a high-end-positioned property management brand with refined visual production, underwriting-disciplined voice, premium pricing, named-submarket market reporting, and a firewall from Megan's future real estate work that is operational as well as visual; the high-end positioning makes the unit economics more sustainable, the brand position more defensible, and the firewall to Megan's surname more valuable, but it raises the bar on operational execution and requires the first 10 placements to be chosen as brand assets rather than as cash flow.
