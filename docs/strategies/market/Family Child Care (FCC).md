# Family Child Care (FCC)

> Standalone strategy document for the licensed Family Child Care wedge. Companion to [[green-lappe-brand-style-guide|Brand Style Guide]] and [[green-lappe-two-sided-marketplace|Two-Sided Marketplace]].

The FCC wedge is a strategic differentiator, not the launch product. Standard single-family residential management in Snohomish County is the launch product; FCC is the margin-expansion play that closes the second meeting with sophisticated owners.

## 1. What FCC Actually is

FCC stands for Family Child Care, the Washington DCYF licensure category for child care operated out of a residential dwelling by a provider who lives in the home. Two relevant subtypes:

- **Licensed Family Home (LFH)**: up to 12 children, depending on provider qualifications and ratios. Provider must reside in the home. Residential zoning is preserved.
- **In-home / In-family**: smaller scale, license-exempt in some configurations. Not the target for this wedge.

The wedge targets LFH conversions of standard single-family homes in Snohomish County (initially) and King County (expansion), with the provider as the resident-operator and Green Lappe Properties as the landlord and operating partner.

## 2. Why This is a Real Opportunity

### 2.1 Supply-demand Imbalance in PNW Child Care

Snohomish and King County child care supply has not kept pace with demand. Waitlists at center-based daycares run 6–18 months in the corridors targeted for launch. Family child care is the elastic supply that fills the gap when centers are full, but the supply of licensed FCC providers is constrained by housing.

Specifically: a licensed FCC provider needs a home that meets DCYF facility standards (square footage, egress, outdoor play space, fire safety) and is located in a zone where FCC is a permitted use. Many providers rent or own homes that do not meet these requirements and cannot afford the homes that do. The constraint is housing access, not provider capacity.

### 2.2 The Green Lappe Position

Green Lappe Properties is positioned to solve the housing-access constraint:

- The company can identify properties suitable for FCC use (zoning, layout, outdoor space).
- The company can fund capex to upgrade properties to facility standards.
- The company can lease to a licensed provider on terms that survive mortgage covenants, insurance carriers, and zoning rules.
- The company can pre-clear the regulatory friction so the provider arrives at a property that is already ready to license.

No competitor in Snohomish or King County does this in an organized way. The closest analog is Wonderschool, which helps an existing resident open a daycare in their own home. Wonderschool does not source properties for providers. That is the gap Green Lappe fills.

## 3. Economics

### 3.1 Revenue Comparison per Single-family Property

|Mode|Annual gross rent|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|Standard residential lease|`$36,000–$54,000`|Market rent for SFH in target corridors, 2026.|
|FCC-converted lease|`$54,000–$72,000`|Premium rent for property pre-configured for FCC use, longer lease term.|
|FCC-converted lease + services fee|`$84,000–$108,000`|Premium rent plus services arrangement (brand, marketing, training, ongoing support) charged as percent of gross tuition or flat fee.|
|FCC-converted lease + capex coordination|one-time `$5,000–$15,000`|Charged on conversion project, separate from ongoing revenue.|

Numbers are illustrative and need validation against actual market comps and operator economics in the target corridors. The point is the order of magnitude: a successful FCC conversion produces roughly 1.5x to 2x the gross revenue of the same property operated as a standard residential rental, before factoring in conversion capex.

### 3.2 Cost Side

Conversion capex is the gating cost. A property that needs full FCC retrofit (fencing, egress upgrades, fire safety, outdoor play surface, kitchen modifications) can run `$15,000` to `$40,000`. A property that is already substantially compliant might need `$3,000` to `$10,000`. Underwriting must be done property-by-property.

Operating cost on the company side is dominated by:

- Regulatory liaison work (helping the provider through DCYF licensing, county zoning, fire marshal, health department).
- Provider sourcing and vetting.
- Insurance coordination across landlord and operator policies.
- Annual compliance reviews.

These costs are concentrated at conversion and at provider transitions, not in steady-state operation. A well-matched provider in a well-configured property is roughly as much work as a good residential tenant.

## 4. Structural Options

Three viable structures for a Green Lappe FCC property. Same options that appear in prior strategic work, with current preference noted.

### 4.1 Structure A: Direct Lease with Premium Rent

Provider leases the property at FCC-premium rent, runs their own licensed business as a sole proprietor or LLC, pays Green Lappe rent only. Green Lappe is purely a landlord.

- Simplest.
- Lowest revenue capture.
- Least operational burden.
- Suitable for experienced, fully self-sufficient providers.

### 4.2 Structure B: Lease plus Services Agreement

Provider leases the property and separately contracts with Green Lappe for services (brand, marketing, billing support, training, parent acquisition, insurance group rate, ongoing coaching). Services fee is either a percent of gross tuition (10–15%, Wonderschool-equivalent) or a flat monthly amount.

- Higher revenue capture than Structure A.
- Moderate operational burden.
- Suitable for providers who are licensed and capable but want operational support.
- This is the recommended default structure.

### 4.3 Structure C: PropCo / OpCo Split

Green Lappe Properties (real estate entity) leases to Green Lappe Childcare LLC (operating entity). OpCo employs the licensed providers and runs the daycare programs. The two entities transact at arm's length.

- Highest revenue capture.
- Highest operational burden.
- Best liability isolation.
- Cleanest financing posture for lenders.
- Cleanest exit for an eventual sale of either side.
- Reserve for the third or fourth property, after operational competence is proven.

## 5. The Provider Side of the Market

A successful FCC wedge requires a pipeline of qualified providers, not just suitable properties. Providers are sourced through:

1. **WA DCYF Welcome Team referrals**: providers in the pre-licensing pipeline who need a property.
2. **Wonderschool network**: providers Wonderschool has trained who are seeking a home.
3. **Snohomish County FCC Facebook groups, Imagine Institute, STARS training cohorts**: existing providers in inadequate facilities.
4. **Direct recruitment**: Indeed, ZipRecruiter, Craigslist, posting "Licensed Family Child Care Provider, housing included."

Provider qualification minimums:

- Current WA DCYF Licensed Family Home license, or clearly progressing through pre-licensing.
- 2+ years of FCC or center-based experience.
- Clean DCYF MERIT record.
- Clean state and federal background checks.
- Active CPR, first aid, Safe Sleep, Mandated Reporter, STARS training.
- Standard rental screening (credit, prior landlord references, no recent evictions).
- References from parents of children previously in care.

## 6. Regulatory and Insurance Posture

Three things make or break the structure:

1. **Zoning**. FCC is permitted residential use in most Snohomish County residential zones (R-7,200; R-8,400; R-9,600) and in many King County residential zones, but verification is required parcel by parcel. Some HOA CC&Rs attempt to restrict, though WA law generally protects FCC as residential use.
2. **Mortgage covenants**. Residential mortgages sometimes restrict business use by tenants. Investment-property loans are usually more permissive. Verify before signing any FCC lease.
3. **Insurance**. Many landlord policies exclude or limit coverage where the tenant operates a daycare on premises. Carriers known to write this coverage with disclosure: Markel, Philadelphia Insurance, K&K. Get a written binder before lease execution.

## 7. DHM Assessment of the FCC Wedge

Applying the DHM framework from [[dhm-framework|DHM Framework]]:

### D—Delight: `3` (strong)

For providers: the property is pre-configured, the regulatory friction is reduced, the housing barrier is removed. This is not a normal landlord relationship; it is a partnership that solves the constraint that holds the provider back. Strong delight signal.

For owners: revenue per square foot meaningfully higher than standard residential, with a longer-term tenant, in a tenancy structured for stability. Owners who care about yield rather than simplicity will recognize this as a category they did not know existed.

### H—Hard To Copy: `3` (strong)

The hardest-to-replicate assets:

- Accumulated knowledge of zoning, mortgage, and insurance constraints across specific corridors.
- Relationships with DCYF licensing staff, county PDS staff, fire marshals, and inspectors.
- A vetted provider pipeline.
- A vetted property pipeline.
- Operational templates (lease addenda, services agreements, compliance checklists) refined through actual conversions.

A national rollup cannot replicate this in Snohomish County without years of local work. A local competitor cannot replicate it without committing to the specialty. The moat is process and tacit knowledge, the hardest kind to copy.

### M—Margin: `3` (strong)

Per-property revenue 1.5x to 2x standard residential, with a longer tenancy and lower turnover cost. Capex amortizes across multi-year leases. Services fee compounds the per-property economics. Margin is materially better than the volume product.

### DHM Verdict: Pursue

Score: `3-3-3`. Concentrate resources accordingly. This is the strategic differentiator that justifies the brand's existence beyond commodity property management.

## 8. Risks Specific to the FCC Wedge

|Risk|Mitigation|
|---|---|
|Provider lapses license mid-tenancy|Cure period in lease, right to terminate, defined notice obligations.|
|Provider vacates, no replacement|Property reverts to standard residential lease; revenue floor is residential market rent.|
|Insurance carrier non-renewal|Carrier-shop at policy level before signing; maintain alternate quotes annually.|
|Mortgage covenant breach|Verify covenants before each FCC lease; refinance to investment loan if needed.|
|Regulatory shift (DCYF rule changes)|Maintain counsel and DCYF Welcome Team relationships; monitor rulemaking.|
|Child injury liability spills to landlord|Provider's insurance with named-additional-insured status; Green Lappe carries umbrella; PropCo / OpCo split for material scale.|
|Property tax reclassification to mixed-use|Confirm with County Assessor before signing; FCC generally preserves residential classification but verify.|
|HOA challenge|WA law protects FCC as residential use; HOA can regulate signage and parking only. Documented review at acquisition.|
|Reputational damage from a bad operator|Provider screening minimum standards; ongoing oversight; immediate termination protocol for safety violations.|

## 9. The Wedge in the Marketplace Framing

In the two-sided marketplace from [[green-lappe-two-sided-marketplace|Two-Sided Marketplace]], FCC introduces three additional participant types:

- `licensed-fcc-provider` on the demand side (already documented).
- `daycare-parent` as a third-order demand signal (already documented).
- `fcc-suitable-owner` as a supply-side subsegment, distinct from `accidental-landlord` because the property has been evaluated for FCC viability.

The wedge does not require its own marketplace. It is a higher-value match within the same marketplace, governed by the same rulebook with an FCC-specific addendum.

## 10. Open Questions for FCC Strategy

1. Sub-brand decision: Green Lappe Childcare as a sister brand, or single brand for both lines.
2. Whether to take an equity position in operator businesses or stay purely contractual.
3. Whether to operate any property as a Green Lappe-employed model (Structure C from day one) versus all-Structure-B at launch.
4. Number of FCC properties to target in year one. Recommendation: `1`, with a target of `3` by end of year two.
5. Whether to actively acquire properties for FCC conversion or limit to owner-supplied inventory.
6. Pricing of services fee: percent of gross tuition versus flat. Tradeoffs for both parties.
7. Whether to publish FCC capability on the public site at launch or hold it as a second-meeting reveal.
