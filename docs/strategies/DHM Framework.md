# DHM Framework

> Standalone reference document. Other documents in this project reference DHM as a shared vocabulary.

## What it is

DHM is a three-part test for whether a product or business strategy is worth pursuing. Originated by Hugh Molotsi (former VP at Intuit and co-author of _The Intrapreneur's Journey_) and popularized by Scott Cook, the framework asks three questions of any strategic bet:

1. **D—Does it delight customers?**
2. **H—Is it hard for competitors to copy?**
3. **M—Does it improve margins?**

A strategy that scores well on all three has a defensible path. A strategy that scores on only one or two is incomplete and will be eroded by competition, by customer indifference, or by economics.

## The Three Tests in Detail

### D—Delight

Delight is the gap between what customers expect and what they get. Meeting expectations is satisfaction. Exceeding them, repeatedly and in ways that matter to the customer, is delight.

Delight is measured by behavior, not by surveys. The signals:

- Unprompted referrals.
- Voluntary renewals at full price.
- Customers who tolerate small failures because the overall relationship is good.
- Customers who pay a premium versus the obvious alternative.

A bet that does not delight produces churn, price pressure, and a relationship that the customer is constantly auditing.

### H—Hard To Copy

A bet that delights customers but is easy to copy gets copied. The competitor with more capital or more distribution wins the second round.

Hard-to-copy assets fall into a small set of categories:

- **Accumulated data**: a network or company has data that took years to gather and cannot be shortcut.
- **Network effects**: the product gets better as more participants join.
- **Brand and trust**: built through years of consistent behavior, not marketing spend.
- **Process and tacit knowledge**: how the company actually executes, which is hard to write down and harder to transplant.
- **Regulatory or licensing position**: certifications, licenses, or relationships that take years to build.
- **Switching costs**: integration depth that makes leaving expensive for the customer.

A feature is not a moat. A patent is rarely a moat. A first-mover position is almost never a moat by itself.

### M—Margin

A bet that delights customers and is hard to copy but does not produce attractive margins is a charity, not a business. Margin can come from:

- **Premium pricing** sustained by delight and switching costs.
- **Unit-cost advantage** from scale, automation, or geography.
- **Volume** at acceptable per-unit margin, where the fixed costs are recovered across enough transactions.
- **Adjacent revenue** that the core relationship makes possible.

Margin is the test that kills the most attractive-sounding bets. A strategy that requires sustained subsidy to retain customers is not a strategy.

## How to Use it

DHM is a screening tool, not a planning tool. Apply it to:

- A proposed new product or feature.
- An entry into a new market.
- A pricing change.
- A strategic partnership.
- An acquisition.

The output is a one-page assessment per bet, with each letter scored, the reasoning behind the score, and a clear verdict: pursue, modify, or drop.

## Scoring Rubric

For each of D, H, M, score on a four-point scale:

|Score|Meaning|
|---|---|
|`0`|Negative. The bet damages this dimension.|
|`1`|Weak. The bet does not meaningfully improve this dimension.|
|`2`|Adequate. The bet contributes here but is not differentiated.|
|`3`|Strong. The bet creates a durable advantage on this dimension.|

A bet should score at least `2` on all three letters and at least one `3` to be worth pursuing. Bets that score `3-3-3` are rare and worth concentrating resources on.

## Common Failure Modes

|Failure mode|What it looks like|DHM diagnosis|
|---|---|---|
|The feature trap|A delightful feature competitors copy in three months.|High `D`, low `H`.|
|The moat without a customer|A defensible asset (license, patent, data) nobody pays for.|High `H`, low `D`.|
|The charity|Customers love it, competitors cannot copy it, no one makes money.|High `D`, high `H`, low `M`.|
|The race to the bottom|Commodity product with no differentiation, competing on price.|Low `D`, low `H`, declining `M`.|
|The vanity bet|Founder loves it, customers do not.|Low `D` (regardless of `H` and `M`).|

## How DHM Relates to Other Frameworks

- **vs Porter's Five Forces**: DHM is the company's view; Porter is the industry's view. Use Porter to understand the field, DHM to evaluate the play.
- **vs Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)**: JTBD answers what the customer is hiring the product to do. DHM tests whether the resulting bet is worth pursuing. JTBD feeds DHM.
- **vs OKRs**: OKRs are execution. DHM is selection. The DHM verdict determines which bets get OKRs.
- **vs Lean Canvas / Business Model Canvas**: Lean Canvas describes the model. DHM judges it.
