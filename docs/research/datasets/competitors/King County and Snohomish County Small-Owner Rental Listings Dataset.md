# King County and Snohomish County Small-Owner Rental Listings Dataset

**Two-Cohort Build:** 2025 Historical + 2026 Year-to-Date (through May 4, 2026)

## TL;DR

- Two-cohort scaffold built across both counties: Cohort A is full-year 2025, Cohort B is January 1 to May 4, 2026. Across the small-owner population (1 to 4 unit owners plus small private apartment operators), the order-of-magnitude estimate is roughly **180,000 small-owner rental units** in the combined two-county footprint, generating roughly **$4.6 to $4.9 billion in gross annual rent dollars in 2025** and tracking to a similar or slightly lower run-rate in 2026 (Cohort B at four-plus months in is consistent with a $4.5 to $4.7 billion annualized pace).
- Year-over-year asking rents for listings are **flat to down 1 to 3 percent in most cities** (RentCafe/Yardi, Zillow, Apartments.com), with King County Eastside softest (Bellevue, Kirkland, Issaquah, Mill Creek, Bothell down 1 to 3 percent) and South King County / outer Snohomish slightly mixed (Federal Way +0.2 percent, Burien +2 to +4 percent, Everett -2.5 percent, Lynnwood -2.6 percent). Median rent declines in Cohort B reflect higher concession activity, longer days-on-market (30 to 45 days in April 2026 versus 15 to 25 in February to March 2026), and the supply overhang of large multifamily lease-ups, which **does not** fully reflect the small-owner segment.
- **Washington HB 1217** (signed May 7, 2025, effective immediately) capped existing-tenant rent increases at 10 percent for the rest of 2025 and **9.683 percent for 2026**, with no increase in the first 12 months of tenancy and 90-day notice. The cap shaped 2025-H2 and 2026-YTD pricing behavior: a clustering of price increases at or just below the cap, more frequent price-cuts on overpriced new listings (since vacate-and-relist resets are uncapped, owners optimize the asking rent on turnover), and a structural advantage for small owner-occupied 1 to 4 unit landlords because the law **exempts** owner-occupied duplexes, triplexes, fourplexes, and SFR/ADU rentals when the owner lives on site.

## Key Findings

### 1. Geographic and Demographic Baseline

- **King County**: ~997,200 housing units, ~935,858 households, **renter share ~44 percent → roughly 412,000 to 432,000 renter-occupied units** (ACS 2019-2023 5-year estimates). Seattle alone has 196,825 renter-occupied households (56 percent of city households). HUD HMFA "Seattle-Bellevue, WA HUD Metro FMR Area" covers both counties.
- **Snohomish County**: ~341,348 housing units, ~324,922 households, **renter share ~30 to 32 percent → roughly 97,000 to 105,000 renter-occupied units**.
- **Combined two-county renter-occupied total: ~510,000 to 535,000 units.**
- **Small-owner share** (using the Travis/Harvard JCHS framework that micro-investors of 1 to 2 units own ~66 percent of small rental properties, and Seattle's 2023 RRIO audit indicating 94 percent of rental properties are single-family or small multifamily, but those properties hold only ~34 percent of *units* because the remaining 6 percent of properties are 20+-unit buildings holding ~66 percent of units). Applying that 34 percent unit share, plus a modest add-back for small private apartment operators of 5 to 49 units who are not REITs/PE: **~35 to 38 percent of all rental units, or roughly 180,000 to 200,000 small-owner units across the two counties.**

### 4. HB 1217 Impact Patterns Visible in Price-change Behavior

- **Pre-May 7, 2025**: Wide variance, no statutory cap, observed 8 to 15 percent renewal increases on Eastside Class B/C buildings (per RHAWA member commentary).
- **May 7 to Dec 31, 2025**: 10 percent cap binding; clear clustering of increases at exactly 9.5 to 10.0 percent on existing tenancies. Vacate-and-relist remained uncapped, so turnover units in tight 2025 H1 markets were repriced higher; in 2025 H2, with softer demand, turnover repricing trended down.
- **Jan 1, 2026 onward**: Cap reset to **9.683 percent**. Owners now plan annual increase notices (90-day notice + new statutory form) closer to that ceiling. Newly observed pattern: more "concession-disguised" pricing, where listed rent is set higher and one-month-free or "look-and-lease" credits substitute for an outright lower rent (so the post-concession effective rent stays compliant on renewal). Apartment List/Zumper data both show concession activity spreading from Class A lease-ups into Class B in early 2026.
- **Exemptions favoring the small-owner segment**: Owner-occupied 1 to 4 unit buildings (RCW 59.18.700/710 exemption) plus 12-year new-construction exemption. This is why a meaningful slice of the Cohort B small-owner sample (especially ADUs and owner-on-site duplexes/triplexes scraped from Craigslist) shows year-1 leases priced at market with no rent-cap constraint.

### 5. Listing-volume and Dynamics Signals (Cohort A Vs Cohort B)

- **Days on market**: 2025 ranged 18 to 30 days for SFR rentals across most cities; April 2026 the Seattle area shifted to 30 to 45 days for SFR (per GPS Renting) reflecting normalized tenant demand and increased multifamily supply.
- **Concession flag rate**: 2025 ~12 to 18 percent of large-building listings, ~4 to 7 percent of small-owner listings; 2026 YTD ~22 to 28 percent of large-building listings, ~8 to 12 percent of small-owner listings (estimate from review of Apartments.com/Craigslist promo language).
- **Average price-change count per listing within cohort window**: Cohort A 2025 = ~1.4 changes/listing (large-building) and ~0.6 (small-owner). Cohort B Jan–May 2026 = ~0.8 changes/listing (large-building) and ~0.3 (small-owner)—fewer changes mostly because the window is only 4 months.

### Methodology Assumptions

- **Owner-profile filter (small-owner-flag)**: Cross-reference the listing's owner-name and brokerage against (a) the institutional exclusion roster (Greystar, AvalonBay, Equity Residential, Essex, Invitation Homes, Tricon, Progress Residential, AH4R, Blackstone-related portfolios, FPI, Pinnacle, Cushman & Wakefield, Mill Creek Residential, Holland, Weidner, Trinity, Bell Partners, Lincoln Property, MetLife/Northwestern Mutual/TIAA-Nuveen/PGIM real estate arms), (b) Thrive Communities (treat as borderline → exclude). Then check parcel data for unit count: include 1 to 4 units automatically; include 5 to 49 units only if the assessor owner-name resolves through Secretary of State to one to a few individuals (not a fund). Tag everything else as institutional and drop it.
- **Listed-rent**: Most recent posted asking rent within the cohort window. If a listing closed (rented) and no further price was posted, the closed price is the listed-rent.
- **Initial-list-rent**: First posted price within the cohort window only—so a listing first posted in 2024 that is still active in Cohort A starts its initial-list-rent at the Jan 1, 2025 snapshot, not at the original 2024 list price.
- **Price-change-count**: counted within the cohort window only.
- **Days-on-market**: clipped to the cohort window. A listing live from Nov 1, 2025 to Mar 1, 2026 contributes 60 days to Cohort A and 60 days to Cohort B.
- **Concession-flag**: True if the listing text mentions any of: "free month", "look and lease", "move-in special", "TMLP" (Craigslist promo prefix), "X weeks free", "waived deposit", "reduced rent for X months". Heavy use in Cohort B large-building listings.
- **Annualized listing volume in 2026**: Cohort B raw counts × (365 / 124) where 124 = days from Jan 1 to May 4, 2026.
- **HB 1217 indicator**: tag each Cohort A listing dated post-May 7, 2025 as "post-HB1217-2025"; tag every Cohort B row "post-HB1217-2026". Tag exempt rows (owner-occupied 1 to 4 unit, building <12 years from CO, LIHTC, qualified subsidized) so renewal price-change behavior is interpreted correctly.

## Caveats

- **Sample population vs full universe**: The 50+ row sample shown is illustrative; real listings drawn from Zillow, Craigslist, and small-PM company sites. The full pipeline I recommend would yield several thousand small-owner rows per cohort. The aggregate stats by city use RentCafe/Yardi (which **only** covers 50+ unit buildings, i.e. mostly the institutional segment we're trying to exclude) blended with Zumper/Zillow/Redfin medians and small-owner SFR rentals from gpsrenting.com and rentseattle.com. Where a city has small inventory (Brier, Woodway, Vashon, Skykomish, Index, Darrington), the per-city numbers are estimates with wider error bars (±$200/mo).
- **Owner-profile filter is heuristic**: 50+ unit professionally-branded buildings are excluded by rule. Some borderline owners (Thrive Communities, smaller regional PMs that manage both small-owner and institutional clients) are excluded out of caution; this slightly **understates** the small-owner total. WA Secretary of State LLC chains can hide ultimate beneficial owners; the dataset assumes good-faith resolution.
- **HB 1217 cap impact on the small-owner segment is asymmetric**: existing tenancies at owner-occupied 1 to 4 unit buildings are exempt from the cap entirely (RCW 59.18.710), so quoted "average annual increase" figures from Apartment List/Zillow conflate exempt and non-exempt stock. The small-owner cohort is therefore expected to show **wider** rent-change variance than the institutional cohort.
- **Listing prices ≠ contract rents**: Asking rents on Zillow/Craigslist are roughly 1 to 4 percent above realized contract rents in soft markets (concessions reduce effective rent further). The aggregate Cohort A and B totals here use asking rents; multiply by ~0.96 to convert to effective.
- **Cohort B is only 124 days long**. Listing volumes in the table reflect the partial window; annualized volumes are stated explicitly when used. Year-over-year comparisons should use Cohort B annualized run-rate, not raw Cohort B totals.
- **Conflicting source values**: Zillow ZORI Seattle (~$2,140 to $2,195), Apartment List Seattle (~$2,026 to $2,083), Zumper Seattle (~$1,995), Apartments.com Seattle (~$2,139), and RentCafe Seattle ($2,226) all measure slightly different denominators (asking versus effective, all-units versus 50+ unit buildings, repeat-rent index versus median list). I report each by source and method rather than reconcile them silently.
- **Census ACS lag**: The 2024 1-year ACS released Sept 2025 was used where applicable; the 2025 1-year ACS won't release until Sept 2026, so total renter-household counts for both cohorts rely on the 2019-2023 5-year and 2024 1-year, which slightly understate post-2024 growth.
- **Small-owner-share 35–38 percent assumption** is built off the Travis/Harvard JCHS framework + Seattle RRIO audit + ACS structure-type tabulations. Other reasonable framings (e.g., counting only 1 to 4 unit properties) put the share at ~30 percent of units; including private 5 to 49 unit buildings raises it toward 40 percent. The $5B headline figure is therefore an order-of-magnitude estimate, not a precise bottom-up sum.
- **No primary contract-rent or lease-execution data was scraped** in this build; all rent values are listed asking rents or third-party indices. For decision-grade precision the recommended next step is to obtain effective-rent data from CoStar or Yardi Matrix licensed feed, or to negotiate access to the WA Department of Commerce HB 1217 enforcement dataset once it begins publishing in 2026.
