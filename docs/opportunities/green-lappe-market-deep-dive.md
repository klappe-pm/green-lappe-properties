# Green Lappe Market Deep Dive

Audience: Megan Green. Purpose: partner decision support. Built from the local repo corpus only; no web search used.

## 1. Executive Summary

The Green Lappe opportunity is real, but the strongest launch framing is narrower than the broad market numbers. The two-county small-owner rental market is large enough to support many strategies: the broad corpus model estimates 302,000 small-owner rental units, $7.636B in annual rent roll, and $763.6M of full-stock annual PM TAM at a 10% management fee. The practical near-term PM market is smaller but still ample: the reconciled market memo sizes King County at $185M, Snohomish County at $34M, and the two-county base at $219M annually.

Those numbers are not the reason to launch. They establish that the pond is large enough. The actual reason to launch is a narrower, more actionable wedge: small-owner and accidental-landlord single-family homes in Megan's trust graph, starting in Snohomish County and adjacent north King corridors. The first proof should be 2-5 homes in year one, not 700 doors. The company can then earn the right to expand.

The key strategic shift is to stop pitching Green Lappe as "another PM company" and frame it as an owner-operated operating system for small portfolios. Megan supplies the local trust, property judgment, vendor relationships, and owner acquisition path. Kevin supplies the systems layer: compliance, owner reporting, workflow automation, and evaluation discipline. The partnership only works if the systems layer actually preserves Megan's time and improves service quality. That is the central assumption to test.

## 3. Market Fragmentation and Entry Conditions

The market memo estimates the top five in-scope King County operators at roughly 6,180 doors combined, or about 11% of SAM. No single operator exceeds 7% of SAM. The remaining managed doors are spread across many independent operators, franchises, and tech-forward entrants.

This matters because Green Lappe is not trying to unseat a monopoly. The opportunity is a services market with:

- many small operators;
- aging founder-led shops;
- fee structures that customers do not trust;
- high compliance burden;
- low operational visibility;
- owner dissatisfaction around maintenance and statements;
- staff burnout that makes service quality hard to sustain.

The corpus also identifies M&A optionality: 50-300 door tuck-ins at $400-$800 per door are a standard exit path in the memo's framing. That does not mean the launch should be acquisition-led. It means the eventual book has a path to value even if Green Lappe stays capped and partner-operated.

## 4. The Pain Is a Design Input, Not the Pitch

The pain-point docs are unusually rich. They show three personas harmed by the same broken operating model:

| Persona | Dominant pain | Green Lappe implication |
|---|---|---|
| Owner/small landlord | Opaque fees, slow responses, vendor markups, regulatory landmines, exit friction. | Publish fee posture, enforce owner approval thresholds, show source invoices, build compliance as a product. |
| Resident | Maintenance silence, deposit disputes, surprise fees, hostile communication. | SLA-visible maintenance, shared condition documentation, plain-language resident communication. |
| PM staff | Overload, on-call burnout, confusing compliance, low pay against high responsibility. | Do not recreate the incumbent staffing model; use workload caps and systems leverage. |

The deck should not spend too many slides re-litigating the pain. Megan already understands it. The pain matters because it defines the product standards:

- same-business-day owner acknowledgement;
- visible maintenance SLA;
- pass-through vendor invoices;
- no undisclosed maintenance markup;
- clean owner packet;
- move-in/move-out photo evidence;
- jurisdiction-aware notice workflow;
- workload model before adding doors.

## 5. Why Snohomish First

The effort-6 PM sequencing score ranks 98052 Redmond, 98109 Seattle, 98121 Seattle, 98004 Bellevue, and 98101 Seattle as top PM markets. That is useful for expansion, but it should not override the launch memo.

The launch memo says Snohomish County first, single-family first, Megan network first. That is a better execution decision for four reasons:

1. Megan's relationship density is stronger in Snohomish and adjacent north King corridors.
2. The first operating model should be tested where the regulatory surface is cleaner than Seattle proper.
3. Single-family homes map to Megan's transaction history and to the founding Bothell property.
4. FCC conversion optionality is preserved by starting with SFR inventory.

This is the core tradeoff: the highest-ranked PM density markets are not necessarily the best proof markets. The proof market should maximize learning speed, owner trust, and operational control.

## 6. SOM: The Partner-Scale Version

The market memo contains venture-style SOM scenarios:

| Scenario | Y3 doors | Y3 revenue | Y5 doors | Y5 revenue |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Bootstrapped | 200 | ~$770K | 400 | ~$1.5M |
| Funded boutique | 700-1,000 | $2.7M-$3.85M | 1,800-2,500 | $7M-$9.6M |
| Funded + M&A | 1,200-1,400 | $4.6M-$5.4M | 3,000-3,500 | $11.5M-$13.5M |

Those are market-capacity scenarios, not current Green Lappe operating plans. For Megan, the relevant SOM is staged:

| Stage | Door target | Purpose | Success condition |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Proof cohort | 2-5 | Prove service standard and legal/operating setup. | 100% owner retention, 95% habitability SLA adherence, zero fee disputes. |
| Phase 2 | 6-12 | Prove repeatability across a small portfolio. | Onboarding under 30 days; no unmanaged escalation pattern. |
| Capacity-test | 25-50 | Prove Megan's owner-facing capacity and Kevin's systems leverage. | Workload model holds; no degradation in owner/resident response. |
| Steady-state boutique | 150-250 | Deliberate capped business if the model works. | Contribution margin and service quality both hold. |

The pitch prep memo sketches a steady-state case: 200 doors at roughly $3,900 annual revenue per door equals about $780K annual revenue. At 250 doors and roughly $4,000 per door, revenue approaches $1M. These numbers are plausible enough to motivate the work, but the capacity model is not yet proven.

## 7. Pricing and Economics

The corpus documents the incumbent pricing range:

- 8%-12% monthly management fee;
- 50%-100% of one month's rent as leasing fee;
- renewal fees from flat amounts to a percentage of rent;
- maintenance markup from 0%-15%;
- setup fees of $200-$500.

Green Lappe's brand promise points away from undisclosed markups and toward transparent pricing. There are two credible launch choices:

### Option A: Flat-Fee Premium

Owners pay a flat monthly fee per property plus a clearly capped placement fee. This best matches the transparency promise. It also performs well in higher-rent Eastside and Snohomish SFR segments because revenue no longer rises mechanically with rent.

Risk: if the fee is set too low, high-maintenance homes consume margin.

### Option B: Premium Percent, No Markup

Owners pay a 10% management fee and a clearly stated placement fee, with no maintenance markup and no surprise renewal economics. This is easier to compare against incumbents and may feel familiar to owners.

Risk: the model still looks like commodity PM unless the service standards and owner packet are visibly different.

The next decision should not be made in the abstract. It should be tested in 20 discovery conversations with Megan's owner network.

## 8. The Systems Leverage Assumption

The strongest and riskiest claim is that two principals can provide a better service without staffing linearly against doors. The corpus says this is the deck's central gap. It should remain a gap until proven.

The systems roadmap should focus on five workflows:

1. Compliance calendar and notice generation.
2. Owner reporting packet.
3. Maintenance intake, photo capture, vendor dispatch, and SLA tracking.
4. Owner inquiry triage and response drafting.
5. Inspection workflow and deposit-defensible documentation.

The first artifact should be something Megan can show an owner. The strongest candidates are:

- an owner monthly packet prototype;
- a Washington compliance calendar with jurisdiction rules;
- a maintenance SLA dashboard and work order template.

The right proof artifact creates trust and also tests whether Kevin's contribution reduces Megan's operating load.

## 9. FCC Wedge

The FCC wedge is strategically important but should be sequenced after core PM proof. The FCC memo recommends Structure B: lease plus services agreement. It describes materially higher revenue capture than a standard lease with moderate operational burden.

What makes FCC attractive:

- scarce provider-ready properties;
- regulatory and insurance knowledge moat;
- longer lease potential;
- premium rent or services fee potential;
- clear differentiation from commodity PM firms.

What makes FCC risky:

- provider licensing;
- insurance;
- mortgage covenants;
- HOA restrictions;
- child injury liability;
- reputational exposure from a bad operator.

The right partner framing is: preserve FCC as the margin wedge, do not make it the launch product unless an unusually qualified first property/provider pair appears.

## 11. Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Licensing gate | No firm without a compliant designated broker path. | Confirm first, before sales motion. |
| Key-person risk on Megan | The brand and distribution rely on her trust capital. | Backup broker path, operating continuity plan, documented owner communication standards. |
| Kevin remote role | Physical property work needs local coverage. | Vendor escalation tree, Megan local ownership, documented inspection cadence. |
| Capacity overclaim | A two-person model can break if the work is underestimated. | Door-count workload model and staged growth caps. |
| Compliance miss | HB 1217 and local rules create brand-defining failure modes. | Compliance calendar, counsel review, human approval before notices. |
| Fee-position ambiguity | Transparency promise fails if pricing is fuzzy. | Publish fee schedule before launch. |
| FCC liability | Higher-margin wedge brings different risk class. | Separate addenda, insurance binders, provider screening, defer until core PM works. |

## 13. Bottom Line

The market is large, fragmented, and pain-rich. That alone is not enough. Green Lappe becomes compelling if the launch stays narrow enough to prove the promise and if the partnership turns Megan's trust capital plus Kevin's systems leverage into measurable operating quality. The recommended next move is a 90-day proof sprint, not a broad launch.
