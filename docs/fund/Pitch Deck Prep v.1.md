# Pitch Deck Prep v.1

Evaluation of the existing project corpus against what a defensible pitch deck and opportunity narrative need, anchored to the actual operating model: a two-person team with no plans to scale headcount, comprising Megan Green (established residential real estate operator in King and Snohomish counties) and Kevin Lappe (remote operator in Newport Beach, CA, with 15 years in developer platforms and product leadership). Core hypothesis: deliver a higher-NPS, higher-retention property management service to a defined ceiling of doors with two principals leveraging modern systems, at materially better unit economics than incumbents who staff against doors linearly. Long-term exit conversation is in scope.

## The Reframe: This is Not a Typical PM Pitch

The market sizing the project has produced was scoped to a venture-style or boutique-roll-up framing. The actual company is something different and that difference is the strategic asset, not a limitation. The deck has to invert the standard PM growth narrative.

- **Not a roll-up.** Headcount caps. SOM scenarios B and C from the project's market research (700 to 1,400 doors at year 3, requiring multi-staff operations) do not describe this company.
- **Not a lifestyle business in the dismissive sense.** A capped, exit-aware, technology-leveraged services firm with strong unit economics is a deliberate platform play.
- **The right comparable is not "PM company."** It is "two-person professional services firm with technology leverage." Tax practices, specialist legal boutiques, managed services IT firms, single-broker high-volume real estate teams all scale to $2M to $5M revenue with two principals when the model is built right.
- **The hypothesis is the pitch.** Two people can deliver higher quality at better margins than 15-person incumbent operators because the operational stack is built for leverage from day one, not retrofitted onto a 20-year-old workflow. If true, this is defensible. If false, this is a side project. The deck's job is to make the hypothesis credible and falsifiable.

### The Team Thesis

The combination is specifically suited to this opportunity:

- **Megan is a 21-year-tenured local trust source** with 108 transactions, a 5.0 rating across 27 public reviews, an active book of business currently producing 17 transactions per year, and the staging/design instinct that converts directly into property condition discipline. She is the rare PM founder candidate who arrives with a referral pipeline, vendor network, and customer trust already capitalized.
- **Kevin is a 15-year-tenured product operator** whose entire career has been building systems that let smaller teams ship more. He has personally led work reducing a 24-day workflow to 14 days at Google, deploying from two weeks to 15 minutes at Capital One, and building the first product team at a seed-stage regulated-services startup. His skill set is exactly the technology leverage layer the hypothesis requires.
- **The division of labor is naturally defensible**. Megan handles all customer-facing trust work (owner conversations, property assessments, the named accountability the brand requires). Kevin handles the systems, vendor management workflows, accounting integration, document automation, compliance monitoring, owner reporting, and the AI-assisted operational layer that turns one Megan into the throughput of three Megans.

This is a deck-defining advantage. Most PM startups have to spend year one buying local trust through paid acquisition and time. This team starts year one with trust capital that took 21 years to build.

> Two principals can manage 150 to 300 doors at higher owner NPS, longer renter tenure, and 40% to 60% better contribution margin than incumbent King and Snohomish PM firms by leveraging AI-assisted compliance monitoring, automated owner reporting, structured vendor workflows, and a referral acquisition model that does not require a sales team. ## The Gaps, Ranked by Deck Criticality

### Gap 1: the Door Capacity Claim and Its Math

**Status: critical, missing entirely.**

The deck's central numerical claim must be the maximum number of doors two people can manage at the promised quality standard. The corpus has no model for this. The PNW pain points research suggests incumbent maintenance coordinators burn out around 100 to 125 doors each. The operations plan suggests a maintenance coordinator hire at 50 doors. Neither of those applies to a model with software-leveraged automation and a named-accountability brand promise.

The unanswered question: what is the per-door annual hour load on each principal under the proposed operating model, and at what door count does either principal hit a defensible 40-hour week?

Needed: a workload model. Inputs:

- Hours per door per month for routine operations (rent collection, owner statement review, vendor coordination, inspection scheduling, lease renewal preparation): probably 30 to 90 minutes per door per month at steady state.
- Hours per door per turnover event (showing, screening, lease prep, move-in inspection, move-out, deposit accounting): probably 8 to 20 hours per turnover, occurring roughly every 24 to 30 months per door.
- Hours per door per year for compliance work (HB 1217 notices, lease updates, city registrations, annual reviews): 2 to 6 hours per year.
- Hours absorbed by the long tail (evictions, disputes, emergency events): variable, model with a frequency rate.
- Hours per new owner acquisition (Megan's conversations, onboarding, document collection): 8 to 20 hours.

Output: a door-count-versus-principal-hours curve, with a stated ceiling, and a stated set of leverage interventions (which tasks Kevin's systems handle, which Megan handles personally, which are outsourced to vendors).

**Without this slide, the entire pitch is a claim with no support.** Most investors will land here first.

### Gap 2: the Technology-leverage Roadmap

**Status: implicit in Kevin's resume; not made explicit anywhere in the project.**

If the hypothesis is that systems make two people equivalent to a 10-person firm, the deck must show what those systems are, what they cost to build, what they do operationally, and in what order they ship. Kevin's experience suggests these are buildable; the project corpus does not list them.

A first-cut list, anchored to the operations plan and the documented pain points:

1. **AI-assisted notice and lease drafting.** Generates compliant HB 1217 notices, 14-day pay-or-vacate, Seattle 180-day rent increase, lease addenda from a structured property and tenant record. Human-reviewed before send (per Kevin's documented experience with human-reviewed automation for high-risk workflows). Eliminates the largest litigation exposure category named in the operations plan.
2. **Owner reporting automation.** The existing PMS produces a property-based statement; owners want consolidated cross-property reporting. Kevin builds a layer over the PMS API that produces the brand's monthly packet (income statement, expense detail, capital improvement log, rent benchmark, retention recommendation) as a single document per owner across all properties.
3. **Vendor workflow with photo capture and SLA enforcement.** Work orders require photo evidence at intake; Kevin builds the intake form, the vendor dispatch logic, the photo-required closeout, and the owner-approval threshold logic. Eliminates the "no photos" failure mode incumbents are publicly attacked on.
4. **Compliance calendar with city-by-city rules engine.** Tracks every Seattle, Bellevue, Kirkland, Redmond, Snohomish city ordinance, generates the right notice form at the right lead time. Decoupled from the PMS so the rules engine can be updated independently when laws change.
5. **Owner inquiry triage.** AI-classifies inbound owner messages by urgency and category, routes Megan's attention to the highest-leverage conversations, drafts responses for routine items. Same-business-day acknowledgement standard becomes operationally enforceable.
6. **Tenant inquiry triage and self-service.** Same pattern on the resident side. Repair requests structured at intake, common questions answered by the system, escalation only for the cases that need a human.
7. **Inspection workflow.** Mobile-first photo capture aligned to the move-in checklist, the 90-day inspection, the 6-month recurring inspection, and the move-out comparison. Deposit accounting generates automatically from the photo evidence.

Each of these maps to documented incumbent failure modes and has clear precedent in Kevin's stated work history.

Needed: a roadmap page showing what ships when, what it replaces, what it costs to build, and what the operational hours saved are.

### Gap 3: Capacity-aware Financial Model

**Status: project has top-line revenue scenarios; no model for a capped operation.**

The existing SOM scenarios assume a growth firm. The actual model is different.

Sketch of the capped-operation P&L logic (numbers illustrative, must be modeled):

- **Door ceiling**: target 150 to 250 doors at steady state (to be confirmed by gap 1).
- **Revenue per door**: $3,400 to $4,300 per year depending on submarket. Megan's geography is Eastside-heavy and Snohomish-corridor; weight toward the upper end. Assume $3,900 blended.
- **Steady-state revenue**: 200 doors × $3,900 = $780,000 per year. 250 doors × $4,000 = $1,000,000 per year.
- **Variable costs at the door level**: PMS subscription ($1.50 to $3.00 per door per month), screening pass-through (zero net), payment processing on cards (passed to tenant), maintenance pass-through (zero markup per brand commitment), insurance per-door allocation.
- **Fixed costs**: E&O and GL ($8K to $15K/year), software stack ($5K to $10K/year), legal retainer ($24K/year), CPA ($10K/year), associations ($2.5K/year), phone ($1.5K/year), marketing minimal (referral-driven), Kevin's tooling cost.
- **Principal compensation**: the residual. This is what the model produces, not what the model assumes.

At 200 doors and $780K revenue with $150K to $200K in fixed costs, principal compensation is $580K to $630K, split between Kevin and Megan according to the partnership agreement. This is the bull case the deck has to defend.

The deeper question: how does Megan's existing brokerage income (17 transactions per year at typical 2.5% commission on $1.1M average = ~$467K gross commission income, less brokerage split) factor into the model? Is the PM business additive to Megan's brokerage income, or does it cannibalize transaction volume? This needs a stated assumption.

**The deck's financial slide should show**: revenue ramp (doors over time), contribution margin per door, fixed cost line, and principal compensation at year 1, year 3, year 5. Cash trough month. Capital required to get to cash-flow positive (probably under $100K given Megan's existing income coverage). Sensitivity on the two highest-impact variables: door ceiling and average fee per door.

### Gap 4: the Exit Narrative

**Status: user mentioned exit conversation is in scope; project has no exit framing.**

For a two-person, capped-headcount business, the exit is the part of the story most investors and partners will probe hardest. The model is unusual enough that the exit path must be made explicit.

Plausible exit scenarios, each requiring different deck treatment:

1. **Strategic sale to a regional PM operator** (Real Property Associates, Bell-Anderson successor, Brink, or a national rollup like PURE Property Management / HomeRiver Group). Typical multiples for PM books of business in this range: $400 to $800 per door at the smaller end, climbing to 1x to 2x annual revenue or 4x to 8x EBITDA at the higher quality end. For a 200-door book at $780K revenue, this implies a $1.5M to $3M exit range under modest assumptions, materially higher if EBITDA margins are at the upper bound the two-person model implies.
2. **Strategic sale to a real estate brokerage seeking PM capability**. Megan's existing FIRST AND MAIN relationship is the obvious first conversation. Other brokerages (Windermere, John L. Scott, Compass) routinely buy PM books to retain agent relationships.
3. **Acquisition by a technology platform** (Doorstead, Mynd, Belong, Roofstock-adjacent) looking for proven operational templates in specific geographies. Less likely but possible if the systems Kevin builds are demonstrably differentiated.
4. **Wind-down with book sale**. Each owner relationship sold individually to a successor PM. Lower aggregate value but no transition risk.
5. **Long-term hold and cash generation**. Not an exit, but a valid alternative that should be named so investors know it's available if the market for buyers isn't there.

The project file `compass_artifact_wf-4a355d66` notes that several incumbent firms are aging founder-led shops (Cornell 1972, Bell-Anderson 1972, Walls 1960s, Pacific Crest 2003) with likely founder retirements in scope. The deck can position the company as the natural rolling acquirer or the natural rolled-up target.

The "(((larger business long-term exit conversation to connect here)))" the user flagged: this almost certainly refers to a conversation already in progress with a specific potential acquirer or partner. That conversation should be named in the deck (under NDA if needed) as the explicit endgame. If a named potential acquirer exists, the deck premise changes: the operation is a deliberate, time-bounded value-creation play with a known exit counterparty, not an open-ended business.

Needed: a one-page exit narrative naming the realistic scenarios, the implied valuations, and (if a specific conversation is in scope) the relationship and timeline.

A defensible estimate: 20% to 35% are likely landlords or future landlords given the price points and submarkets. That's 22 to 38 prospects in the existing book.

Out-of-state buyers are disproportionately likely to need PM. Probably 30% to 50% conversion. That's 5 to 8 doors per year from current transaction flow.

- **Geographic concentration**: Megan's service areas (Edmonds, Lynnwood, Mountlake Terrace, Mill Creek, Kenmore, Shoreline, Woodway, Snohomish, Bellevue, Kirkland) align with the project's stated Snohomish-corridor and Eastside submarkets. This is the same market she has been operating in. The geographic narrative writes itself.
- **The founding property** (Bothell, mentioned in the brand documents) as the proof-of-concept first door.

Needed: a "first 50 doors" page with an attribution model. Best-case 12-month, base-case 18-month, conservative 24-month.

### Gap 7: Validated Demand Evidence

**Status: inferred from Megan's review base, not formally collected.**

The 27 Zillow reviews are owner-side and buyer-side trust evidence, not PM-side demand evidence. The pitch needs:

- **Discovery interviews**: how many owners has Megan spoken to about PM specifically, not transaction services? Even 10 conversations with documented notes is strong evidence.
- **Letters of intent**: how many owners have committed to sign as customers when the firm is licensed? Even 3 named owners with documented door counts changes the deck from a hypothesis to a launch announcement.
- **The founding property**: a public case study of the Bothell property the brand documents reference, with current state (under what manager), pain points, financials, and the proposed transition.
- **Win/loss research with prospective customers**: what would it take to switch them from their current PM? What fee would they pay? What service standards are non-negotiable?

This is the cheapest gap to close. Megan can run 20 of these conversations in 60 days without any business infrastructure.

### Gap 8: the Systems-leverage Proof Point

**Status: missing.**

Kevin's resume claims are credible, but the deck can be materially stronger with a built-and-shipped artifact that proves the technology-leverage hypothesis before launch.

Candidates for a pre-launch proof point:

- **A working compliance calendar and notice generator** for HB 1217, fully tested against current Washington Department of Commerce forms. Could be shared with prospective owners and referral sources as a free utility, generating demand and trust simultaneously.
- **An owner reporting prototype** built on top of a sample PMS export, showing the brand's monthly packet alongside the standard AppFolio statement. The visual difference becomes the sales pitch.
- **A property condition AI assistant** that takes a smartphone walkthrough video and produces a draft move-in checklist, condition photos with timestamps, and a deposit-defensible record. Trivial demo, high signal.

One of these built and demonstrable changes the pitch from "we will build" to "we have built; we are now scaling adoption." The investor calibration is materially different.

### Gap 9: Pricing Strategy Commitment

**Status: pricing range documented in market research; firm's actual pricing not committed.**

The project knows incumbent pricing is 8% to 12% monthly management plus 50% to 100% leasing fee plus various ancillaries. The brand work commits to transparency and no maintenance markup. The actual price point is not stated.

Three plausible pricing structures, each pointing the business at a different segment:

1. **Premium positioning**: 10% management, 75% leasing, no markups, no setup, no renewal fees (the GPS Renting model, extended). Targets owners willing to pay for transparency and quality. Compatible with the brand promise. Most defensible against margin compression.
2. **Subscription model**: flat $199 to $299 per door per month plus 50% leasing. Predictable for the owner, predictable for the firm, structurally favorable to high-rent properties. Differentiator that incumbents structurally cannot match without breaking their fee structure.
3. **Performance pricing**: 8% management, 100% leasing, with a guaranteed days-on-market SLA and a guaranteed eviction prevention rate. Highest risk to the firm, highest defensible margin if the operational claims hold.

The deck needs one committed pricing structure with rationale. Sensitivity analysis on this assumption matters in the financial model.

### Gap 10: Risk Register and Counter-narrative

**Status: scattered through research files; not consolidated.**

The two-person model has specific risks that need direct treatment:

- **Key-person risk on Megan.** Illness, family event, brokerage departure. Mitigation: cross-training Kevin on customer-facing essentials, a named backup designated broker, an explicit operating continuity plan.
- **Kevin geographic distance.** Newport Beach to Seattle is a 2.5-hour flight; physical issues at properties require Megan or a vendor. Mitigation: documented escalation tree, vendor network resilience.
- **Kevin employment conflict.** Google employment terms may restrict outside business activity. Needs to be cleared in writing.
- **The brokerage-PM conflict of interest.** Megan's listing clients may overlap with PM prospects; managing both relationships requires disclosed practices and possibly separate entities. Real exposure under WA real estate law if not handled cleanly.
- **The capped-headcount model has no surge capacity.** A 90-day window of three simultaneous evictions, an HVAC system failure across multiple properties, and a fair housing complaint could exceed two-person bandwidth. Mitigation: outsourced after-hours triage (Latchel), retained eviction counsel, vendor depth.
- **Software dependency.** If the systems Kevin builds break, the model degrades to standard PM with two people, which is uneconomical. Mitigation: vendor selection biased toward stability, redundancy on critical paths.
- **HB 1217 retroactive enforcement** (the AG fined 8 landlords in August 2025 over notice defects). One miss is a brand-defining event. Mitigation: the compliance calendar and notice generator from gap 2.

Needed: a one-page risk register that names each, scores severity and likelihood, and states the mitigation.
