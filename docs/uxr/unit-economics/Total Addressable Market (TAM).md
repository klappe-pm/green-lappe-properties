# Total Addressable Market (TAM)

> Prepared May 2026. All dollar values are nominal current-year USD unless flagged as inflation-adjusted. Estimates labeled MODELED rely on the assumptions stated inline. The analysis is scoped strictly to non-institutional rental owners (individual landlords, family LLCs, small portfolios under approximately 50 units). Institutional REITs, PE-owned, and named national operators are excluded.

---

## TL;DR

- The small-owner addressable rental pool in King and Snohomish counties is approximately **298,000-310,000 occupied units** (roughly **228,000 in King**, **74,000 in Snohomish**), generating an estimated **gross annual rent roll of $7.4 billion to $7.9 billion** (MODELED, 2025 base), of which only an estimated **22-30% is currently under professional third-party management** per RHFS and NARPM benchmarks.
- At a **10% management-fee tier**, the full-stock TAM for an independent property manager today is approximately **$740M-$790M per year combined** (King ≈ $590M, Snohomish ≈ $175M); at **8%** it is **$595M-$635M**, at **6%** it is **$445M-$475M**, at **12%** it is **$890M-$950M**. Adding a turnover-only leasing TAM (one month's rent per turnover) layers an additional **$155M-$195M per year**.
- Twenty-year forecast: under PSRC/OFM baseline rental-stock growth (~1.5% annual) plus the historic Seattle-area rent CAGR of ~4.3% (2015-2025 ZORI), combined small-owner TAM at the 10% fee tier reaches roughly **$1.9B-$2.1B annually by 2046**. Under stress-test scenarios of 5%/8%/10%/12%/15% small-owner inventory CAGR, 2046 TAM at 10% spans **$2.1B (5% growth) to $13.0B (15% growth)**, but the 12% and 15% paths are physically implausible without institutional capital that would dilute the small-owner share, so those upper figures should be read as ceilings, not forecasts.

## Section 2. Methodology, Scope Filter, and Data Sources

### 2.1 Scope Filter Applied

Included: individual landlords; family LLCs; small portfolios (estimated <50 units under common ownership). Excluded: REITs (including Starwood, Equity Residential, AvalonBay, Essex, MAA, Camden, UDR), private equity SFR aggregators (Invitation Homes, AH4R, Progress, Tricon, Amherst, FirstKey, Pretium), large local/regional multifamily operators (Greystar, Weidner Apartment Homes, Pinnacle, Bell Partners), insurance/pension portfolios, and any building of 50+ units under common control.

ACS does not directly tag owner type; RHFS supplies national owner-type distributions by property size that we apply to local ACS unit-count data. ### 8.7 Sensitivity / Plausibility Note

The 12% and 15% scenarios are arithmetic exercises, not forecasts. Achieving 12-15% annual small-owner inventory growth in two counties already producing roughly 2-3% per year (combined ~25,000 net new housing units annually in King, ~6,000-9,000 in Snohomish per OFM 2024-2025 data) would require either: (a) institutional capital building units that are then sold off into small-owner hands (which would reverse the post-2012 institutional consolidation trend and is unlikely given current investor concentration in BFR / SFR aggregators), or (b) a structural conversion of homeowner stock to small-owner rentals at a scale not seen since the 2008-2012 period. **The realistic 20-year envelope sits between Scenario A (5%, $5.2B TAM by 2046 at 10%) on the high end and the PSRC baseline (~$2.8B by 2046 at 10%) on the low end.** Anything above Scenario B (8%) implies institutional builds that would dilute the small-owner share below the 50-58% currently observed.

## Section 12. Data Gaps and Limitations

1. **RHFS does not publish at county level.** Owner-type splits are national; we apply national/regional shares to local ACS unit counts. Confidence: medium-high.
2. **ACS B25032 5-year data lags by 2 years.** 2024 1-year structure data is preliminary; 5-year 2019-2023 used for cross-tabulations.
3. **Assessor parcel-data exclusion of named institutional entities was not exhaustively executed.** A full institutional-exclusion pass on King County Assessor + Snohomish County Assessor parcel data would refine the small-owner share by 1-3 percentage points; fully reconciling LLC-shielded ownership requires WA Secretary of State entity lookups beyond the search-tool scope of this report.
4. **ZORI methodology change in 2024** means pre-2024 ZORI is not strictly comparable to post-2024; Zillow back-cast to 2015 but the 4.3% CAGR has a 30-50 bps uncertainty band.
5. **Effective vs asking rent gap.** ZORI is asking rent; effective rents in late 2024-2025 are 2-4% lower because of widespread concessions (37% of Sept 2025 listings on Zillow include concessions). TAM tables use blended asking-rent assumptions; greenfield revenue would be 2-4% lower if effective rents were used.
6. **Bedroom and bathroom mix at ZIP level not exhaustively enumerated.** ACS does not publish bath-count by tenure × structure type at ZCTA level; assessor records do, but a ZIP-by-ZIP appendix would require direct parcel-data export from both counties.
7. **The 50+ unit small-owner sliver** (9% MODELED) has the most uncertainty; the actual share of 50-99 unit buildings owned by non-institutional family LLCs in King County is somewhere between 5% and 18% based on RHFS 2021 confidence intervals.
8. **Forecast scenarios at 12% and 15% inventory CAGR are not realistic baselines.** They are stress-test ceilings. Realistic 20-year planning should anchor on the 2-5% range.
9. **Rent CAGR will mean-revert.** The 4.3% 2015-2025 CAGR included the post-pandemic surge; future CAGR could be 2.5-3.5% per CoStar / Yardi Matrix outlook.
10. **STR overlay** double-counts a small share of inventory that is also reported as long-term rental in ACS; the STR sub-TAM should be treated as additive only with caution.
11. **Currently-managed share may be higher in Seattle metro** than the national 22% benchmark (HUD Seattle Property Management Fees guides imply 30-35% of small-owner SFR is professionally managed locally because of the regulatory complexity); using 22-25% gives the greenfield numbers a conservative tilt.
12. **PSRC growth allocations are policy targets, not market forecasts.** Actual delivery has run below targets in some periods.

## Caveats

1. **All TAM figures are MODELED outputs, not measured cash flows.** They represent the theoretical maximum management revenue if the entire small-owner pool were under third-party management at the stated fee tier. Actual capturable share is bounded by self-management preference, owner cost-sensitivity, and competition.
2. **Small-owner share is derived, not directly observed.** RHFS provides national distributions; we apply them to local ACS unit counts. A direct King and Snohomish County Assessor parcel-data export with named-institutional-entity exclusion would reduce the uncertainty band on small-owner unit counts from roughly ±8% to ±3%.
3. **2024-2025 rent softening is real.** Zillow ZORI September 2025 shows national multifamily up only 1.7% YoY; Seattle metro is +1.8-2.1%. Single-family rentals are growing 3.2% YoY (Zillow Oct 2025), the smallest since 2016. The 4.3% historical CAGR may overstate the next 5 years; the next 20 years could revert to mean.
4. **The 12% and 15% inventory CAGR scenarios are arithmetic ceilings, not forecasts.** They are mathematically inconsistent with the assumption that the small-owner share holds constant; achieving them would require institutional builds that dilute the small-owner share.
5. **Fee tiers reflect typical ranges, not negotiated rates.** Real Seattle market data clusters at 8-10% for SFR and 5-7% for 5+ unit small multifamily. The 14% and 16% tiers are realistic only for short-term rental management or specialty / luxury segments.
6. **Effective vs asking rent gap of 2-4% currently.** TAM tables are anchored on weighted asking rent; effective collected rent is lower because of widespread concessions in late 2024-2025.
7. **Greenfield share (78% self-managed) is national.** Seattle's regulatory complexity (Just Cause Eviction, FIT, RRIO, 120-day rent-increase notice) likely pushes more marginal owners to outsource than the national average; the actual greenfield could be 65-75% rather than 78%, narrowing the addressable pool by 5-15%.
8. **Owner-type structures** (LLPs, LLCs, family trusts) are increasingly used by individual investors for liability and estate purposes; the "individual" RHFS category understates true small-owner share, while the "LLP/LLC" category overstates institutional share. Chandan Economics 2025 estimates suggest combining the two categories captures the small-owner pool accurately, which is the approach used here.
9. **Short-term-rental TAM should not be added to long-term TAM without careful de-duplication.** Some Airbnb-listed units are seasonally rotated between long-term and short-term; counting them in both inflates TAM by 2-4%.
10. **City-level renter household counts** beyond top-12 are estimated from ACS 5-year 2019-2023 data; small cities (under 5,000 renter households) have wide ACS margins of error.
11. **ZIP-level tables are model outputs, not direct ACS pulls.** Full enumeration of all 90 King and 50 Snohomish ZIPs at ACS B25032 ZCTA geography is feasible via Census API but exceeds search-tool execution scope. The framework above produces deterministic outputs once raw ZCTA data is loaded.
12. **PSRC growth targets are policy goals.** Actual housing delivery in the region has tracked targets within ±15% historically; assuming the baseline 2.0% small-owner inventory CAGR is consistent with PSRC RHNA 2022 if the small-owner share holds.
