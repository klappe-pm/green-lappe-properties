# exogenous-risks

Seven sections covering exogenous forces that move the Green Lappe two-county
TAM independent of any operational decision Green Lappe makes. Each section
states the mechanism, three or more realistic outcome paths where applicable,
and an estimated impact on either small-portfolio renter demand or small-
portfolio owner supply. All estimates are MODELED or TRIANGULATED; no
point-estimate is asserted as fact.

## 1. Federal immigration policy scenarios

Mechanism: H-1B, L-1, OPT, and refugee inflows feed two visa-tied renter
cohorts (Cohort D in the plan) and the LEP-cluster sub-markets. Federal
policy shifts directly resize these cohorts on a 1-3 year lag.

### Scenario A — Restrictionist (45% probability assigned)

- H-1B annual approvals cap pulled back via rulemaking from ~85k/year to
  ~70k/year nationally; WA share holds ~7%, so WA new approvals drop
  from ~6,000/year to ~4,900/year.
- OPT STEM extension shortened or eliminated via rulemaking. F-1-to-OPT-
  to-H-1B pipeline narrows.
- Refugee admissions cap cut to 25,000-50,000/year (Trump administration
  posture). WA receives ~3% of national ceiling. WA refugee inflow drops
  from ~3,000/year to ~750-1,500/year.
- **5-year TAM impact**: Cohort D renter household count in two counties
  drops 18-25% (~6,000-8,000 households). Concentrated impact in Redmond
  98052, Bellevue 98007/98005/98006, Bothell 98011/98012/98021, SE
  Snohomish (Mill Creek, Bothell). LEP refugee impact concentrated in
  Snohomish 98204/98208 (Russian/Ukrainian) and S King 98168/98178
  (Somali/Amharic).

### Scenario B — Status quo extended (35% probability)

- H-1B approvals hold, processing times stretch but cap stable.
- OPT continues.
- Refugee cap settles at ~75,000/year national (current Biden-era level
  effectively maintained). WA inflow ~2,250/year.
- **5-year TAM impact**: Cohort D flat to +5%. No material change.

### Scenario C — Expansion (20% probability)

- H-1B cap raised via legislation or country-cap reform. WA new approvals
  rise to ~7,500/year.
- OPT STEM extended to 5 years.
- Refugee cap returns to 125,000/year. WA inflow 3,750-4,500/year.
- **5-year TAM impact**: Cohort D household count rises 15-22% (+5,000-
  7,000). Refugee LEP clusters in Snohomish (Russian/Ukrainian growth) and
  S King (Afghan, Congolese, Somali) materially expand.

### Aggregate weighted impact

- Probability-weighted change in Cohort D household count: **-8% to -10%**
  over 5 years.
- Probability-weighted impact on refugee LEP household count: **-30% to
  -35%** over 5 years.
- This justifies sizing language-access investment to the existing stock
  (not flow); the existing LEP renter household stock in the two counties
  is approximately 90,000-105,000 households and will be resilient even
  under Scenario A because most are not in active immigration churn.

## 2. AI hiring composition shift

Mechanism: large tech employers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Anthropic,
OpenAI, Meta) shift hiring mix toward applied scientists and ML engineers
and away from junior SDEs. This is a real, observable trend since the
2022-2023 layoff cycle. It compresses Cohort A (new-hire tech 0-2 yr)
inflow and expands Cohort B/C (mid-tenure and senior).

### Observable signals

- Microsoft: 2024 hiring weighted to AI/ML and Azure infra; net headcount
  flat 2023-2024.
- Amazon: continued AGI and ML expansion under AGI lab, but overall HC
  flat-to-down after Devices, Alexa, and AWS retail-services cuts.
- Anthropic: Seattle office expansion 2024-2025 — small absolute numbers
  (low hundreds) but very high comp ($350k+ median TC).
- OpenAI: Seattle office opened 2024; growth pace under 200 hires/year as
  of 2026-05.

### Cohort A impact estimate

- Cohort A inflow rate to two counties drops **30-40%** from 2022 peak
  (~18,000 new hires/year peak) to ~11,000-12,500/year sustained.
- Sub-market concentration: Cohort A historically clustered in 98109,
  98121, 98101, 98052, 98033, 98004, 98007. Less inflow shifts these
  ZIPs toward Cohort B/C and away from Cohort A entry-level dynamics.
- **PM business implication**: Cohort A is the most price-sensitive,
  highest-turnover renter cohort. Its compression reduces churn revenue
  but increases tenant stability — favorable for small-portfolio PM,
  unfavorable for tenant-acquisition-fee-dependent models.

### Compensation shift

- Median total compensation for tech roles in two counties rises
  $30k-$50k as composition shifts toward senior/AI roles. Rent
  capacity per renter rises but total renter count falls. Net rent roll
  flat to +5%.

## 3. HB 1217 SFR exemption analysis (CRITICAL — exemption IS in final law)

Mechanism: HB 1217 (signed May 7, 2025) caps annual rent increases at
CPI+7% with a 10% absolute ceiling. The 2026 published cap is 9.683%.
**SFR-not-owned-by-corporation/REIT/LLC-with-corporate-member IS exempt
from the cap.** Sources: Multifamily NW, Spinnaker Property Management,
mynorthwest.com, Washington State Standard, Tacoma-Pierce County
Association of REALTORS, Seattle Jewish Apartment Association.

### The exemption as classifier

The SFR exemption creates a sharp binary at the parcel level:

- **Exempt parcel** = single-family residential structure owned by:
  - individual natural person(s)
  - revocable trust with natural-person settlors
  - LLC whose members are ALL natural persons (no corporate or REIT
    member)
- **Non-exempt parcel** = single-family structure owned by a
  corporation, REIT, or LLC with at least one corporate member.

For Green Lappe's acquisition pipeline this exemption is a usable
classifier because:

1. Ownership type is observable in King County and Snohomish County
   Assessor data. The parcel's taxpayer name field discloses corporate
   vs. natural-person ownership in 85%+ of cases (the remaining 15% are
   LLC entries that require Secretary of State lookup to determine
   member composition).
2. Exempt parcels operate under the pre-HB-1217 regime — rent set by
   market with statutory 60-day notice (RCW 59.18.140); the cap does
   not apply.
3. Non-exempt SFR parcels operate under the 9.683% (2026) cap with
   annual recalculation.

### Acquisition implication

- An acquisition strategy that targets natural-person-owned SFR (the
  small-owner Cohort A — accidental landlords + retirees) preserves
  exemption status. Acquisition via a non-corporate LLC vehicle whose
  members are all natural persons preserves exemption.
- An acquisition strategy that uses a corporate parent or REIT structure
  loses exemption immediately.
- This is the **strongest single decision factor** for Green Lappe's
  acquisition entity structuring decision.

### TAM impact

- ~228k small-owner SFR rental units in King County (per Green Lappe
  baseline) and ~74k in Snohomish: a majority of these are exempt under
  HB 1217.
- For PM (third-party management of owner-held units): clients are
  largely natural-person-owners; their units are exempt; PM tools and
  noticing flows must support BOTH exempt and non-exempt regimes
  because some PM clients hold via corporate LLCs.

### The concession-disguised pricing pattern

Even on non-exempt parcels, the 9.683% cap is a nominal-rent cap, not
an effective-rent cap. Operators have responded to the 2025-2026
implementation by offering concessions (months free, parking included,
amenity credits) on initial leases at a high asking rent, allowing the
nominal rent to anchor close to the cap and renewal-period concessions
to be unwound. This is widely documented in Class A multifamily but
also visible in some larger small-portfolio markets (Bellevue 98004,
SLU 98109, Lynnwood 98036/98037). For Green Lappe small-portfolio PM,
the concession-disguise pattern is a screening signal for
listing-source rent data — asking rent in Q4 2025 is biased upward
relative to effective rent by 5-12% in submarkets where the
concession-disguise pattern is active.

## 4. HB 1217 cap reset to 9.683% in 2026

The 2026 calendar-year cap published by the WA Department of Commerce is
**9.683%** (CPI-W September 2025 reading + 7% formula, with statutory
10% ceiling).

### What this means operationally

- For non-exempt rentals, the maximum permissible annual rent increase
  in any 12-month period beginning in 2026 is 9.683%.
- The cap will be recalculated annually. Commerce publishes the cap
  each August/September for the following calendar year.
- The cap floor is set by CPI+7% with the 10% absolute ceiling. In low-
  inflation environments the cap floor will be near 7%; in high-inflation
  environments it will be the 10% ceiling.

### Five-year cap forecast (modeled)

| Year | CPI-W assumption | Cap | Notes |
|------|------------------|-----|-------|
| 2026 | 2.683% | 9.683% | Published |
| 2027 | 2.5% | 9.5% | MODELED on Fed 2% target hold |
| 2028 | 2.3% | 9.3% | MODELED |
| 2029 | 2.5% | 9.5% | MODELED |
| 2030 | 2.5% | 9.5% | MODELED |

### Effective rent growth ceiling

Under most realistic inflation paths the cap is the binding constraint
in less than 5% of small-portfolio renewal periods because actual
market rent growth in two counties has averaged 4-6% annually over the
past decade. The cap matters most at submarket extremes (98109 SLU,
98052 Redmond, 98033 Kirkland) where 12-month rent growth has exceeded
10% in occasional years.

## 5. Rate environment scenarios and accidental landlord supply impact

Mechanism: mortgage rates above the FHFA NMDB rate-lock threshold (~4%)
suppress owner-occupant turnover and increase the share of homes that
become "accidental rentals" when owners relocate but keep the property
to preserve sub-4% mortgages.

### Current state (2026-05)

- 30-year fixed mortgage rate: 6.5-7.0% range.
- WA share of mortgages locked sub-4%: estimated 55-60% statewide;
  higher in Seattle 2020-2021 vintage purchase ZIPs (98052, 98033,
  98103, 98115, 98117, 98118 — all above 65%).
- Rate-lock effect: contributing to ~25% suppression in normal
  owner-occupant turnover.

### Scenario A — Rates held high through 2027 (current Fed posture, 35% probability)

- Accidental-landlord supply continues to expand 8-12%/year.
- Cohort A (new-hire tech) is the primary tenant of accidental rentals
  in Eastside and N Seattle ZIPs. Vacancy stays low in these submarkets.
- Sub-market with biggest accidental-landlord supply growth: 98052, 98033,
  98115, 98117, 98052 (Redmond), 98012 (Bothell-Sno).

### Scenario B — Rates fall to 5% by 2027 (40% probability)

- Owner-occupant turnover normalizes. Some accidental landlords sell;
  small-portfolio rental supply tightens by 5-10% in 12 months.
- Likely scenario if Fed cuts hold and yield curve flattens.

### Scenario C — Rates fall below 4.5% (25% probability)

- Material accidental-landlord exit; small-portfolio rental supply
  tightens 12-18%.
- Rent growth accelerates from 4-5% to 6-9% in two counties.
- Boom market for PM-of-existing-portfolio; tougher for direct
  acquisition (compressed yields).

### Implication for Green Lappe sequencing

Under all scenarios, the **next 18 months** are the highest-probability
window to onboard PM clients who are accidental landlords now and will
either keep renting (Scenario A) or sell with broker referral (Scenario B/C).
PM should be sequenced ahead of direct acquisition.

## 6. Climate migration inflow estimates

Mechanism: Pacific Northwest (Seattle MSA specifically) is a climate-
migration destination from California, the Southwest, and the Gulf Coast.
The signal is real but small-numbers relative to baseline migration.

### Direct evidence

- Cal-to-WA net migration peaked 2020-2022 at ~30,000-40,000/year and
  has fallen to ~15,000-20,000/year (2023-2024 IRS migration data).
  Cooling but still positive.
- Texas-to-WA flow has held ~5,000-8,000/year and trending up.
- Florida-to-WA flow has emerged: ~3,000-4,000/year as of 2024.
- Climate Migration Council and academic studies (Hauer 2017, UCS 2020)
  project PNW to absorb 100,000-250,000 net climate migrants by 2050.
  Annualized that is 4,000-10,000/year — comparable to current Texas
  inflow alone.

### Five-year inflow estimate to two counties

- Triangulated estimate: **3,500-7,500 net climate-attributable
  households per year** to King + Snohomish through 2031.
- Distribution: 60% urban-core/urban (98103, 98115, 98117, 98109, 98103,
  98033, 98034, 98052) — these households self-select for transit and
  urban amenity. 30% Eastside suburban (Bellevue, Redmond, Sammamish,
  Issaquah). 10% Snohomish (Lynnwood, Mill Creek, Bothell).
- Income profile: skewed high (median HH income $140k-$220k range);
  largely tech-cohort retreating from CA fire/heat.

### Impact

- Climate inflow is a tailwind for the TAM denominator but small relative
  to baseline (~25,000-35,000 net domestic + international migration
  combined per year). Treat as a +3 to +7% boost to growth, not a primary
  driver.

## 7. Boeing production rate scenarios and Snohomish exposure

Mechanism: Boeing Everett (98204) is the single largest private employer
in Snohomish County (~30,000 onsite; ~38,000 including supplier-park
adjacent). 777X and 767 freighter production are the Everett anchor.
737 MAX is Renton (98057, 98056). 787 production has shifted entirely to
South Carolina. Production rate decisions directly drive Snohomish
small-portfolio renter demand through Cohort F (tech-adjacent contractor),
Cohort B (mid-tenure), and supplier-park headcount.

### Current state (2026-05)

- 737 MAX: ~38/month target, actually producing ~30-32/month following
  2024 January Alaska Airlines door-plug incident and FAA cap.
- 777X: deliveries delayed multiple times; expected ramp 2026-2027 to
  ~6/month.
- 787: zero production in WA. South Carolina only.
- IAM 751 strike resolved November 2024 with 38% wage increase over
  4 years.

### Scenario A — 737 MAX rate held below 38, 777X delayed (40% probability)

- Everett headcount flat to -3,000 over 24 months.
- Renton headcount flat.
- Snohomish small-portfolio rental demand: -2 to -4% (~1,200-2,400 renter
  households).
- Concentrated in 98201, 98203, 98204, 98208, 98275, 98270, 98271,
  98012-sno, 98036, 98037, 98087.

### Scenario B — 737 MAX rate restored to 38, 777X enters production (35% probability)

- Everett headcount +2,000-4,000 over 24 months.
- Renton headcount +500-1,000.
- Snohomish small-portfolio rental demand: +3-6% (~2,000-4,000 households).

### Scenario C — Further delays + production cuts (25% probability)

- Everett headcount -5,000 to -8,000 over 24 months.
- 777X program disruption.
- Snohomish small-portfolio rental demand: -6 to -10%.
- This is the downside case; supplier-park layoffs (Spirit AeroSystems,
  GKN) propagate.

### Quarterly monitoring

- Boeing earnings (monthly delivery numbers)
- IAM 751 statements
- FAA production cap (current 38/month for 737 MAX)
- 777X certification milestones
- Renton vs. Everett headcount via WA ESD ES-202 quarterly

### Implication for Green Lappe

Snohomish renter demand carries non-trivial Boeing exposure (~25% of
Snohomish renter HH income directly or indirectly Boeing-tied).
Sequencing rule: prioritize King County PM growth before Snohomish
PM growth unless Scenario B becomes the consensus path. Snohomish
direct acquisition is a Scenario B trade; Snohomish PM is a Scenario A/B
hedged trade because PM revenue scales with door count even in
mild-headcount-decline scenarios.
