# 2026-07-22-4632-market-anchored-childcare-pro-forma

## purpose-and-boundary

This is the market-anchored companion to the 2026-07-22 Washington childcare license and operating-model decision brief. It takes the brief's discussion-only financial model and re-grounds its two load-bearing inputs, tuition and rent, in current 98012 market data for the calibration property at 4632 146th Pl SE, Bothell, WA 98012. It does not decide zoning, code compliance, insurability, licensable capacity, or profitability. It does not verify any property-specific fact about 4632. Every dollar here is a planning figure, not a quote.

The purpose is narrow: show which parts of the brief's model the market supports, correct the parts it does not, and state what actually decides whether 4632 works as a childcare property.

## inputs-verified-against-market

Two inputs drive the entire pro forma. Both check out against current data.

- Tuition: the brief's discussion card ($2,250 infant, $2,100 toddler, $1,900 preschool) lands within about 2 percent of local 98012 comps in aggregate. It is not invented.
- Rent: a 4-bedroom single-family home in 98012 runs about $3,744 per month (RentEst, 98012-specific). The brief's $3,500 per month ($42,000 per year) lease assumption for 4632 is realistic and slightly conservative.

## cost-anchor-rent-for-4632

The brief's $42,000 per year lease is defensible for a 4-bedroom at 4632.

- 4-bedroom 98012 market: about $3,744 per month.
- 4-plus-bedroom Bothell (March 2026): about $3,913 per month.
- Brief assumption: $3,500 per month.

Implication: if 4632 is a 4-bedroom, the rent line holds and is mildly conservative. If a larger house is needed to reach licensable capacity, market rent rises to roughly $3,900 to $4,500 per month, cutting annual residual by about $3,000 to $12,000 in either model.

Break-even is the finding. ## model-a-family-home-rebuilt

The brief's Model A table priced an independent family home at full center rates. The market does not fully support that, but the downside case in the original draft understated the floor. Verified against Child Care Aware of Washington's *2024 Snohomish County Child Care Data Report* (primary source, DCYF and Cultivate Learning data): the "$1,195/mo county average" this table used as its downside anchor does not match any 2024 age-group figure. Current verified 2024 family-home (FCC) medians for Snohomish County are infant $1,517/mo, toddler $1,400/mo, preschool $1,300/mo, all 9 to 27 percent above $1,195. The same report's center medians (infant $2,105, toddler $1,895, preschool $1,656) track closely with the Kids Corner Bothell comps already used in this doc, which corroborates the center side of the model. Rebuilt at realistic family-home pricing, holding the brief's $82,400 assistant-plus-operating cost:

|---|---:|---:|---:|---|

| 15 percent below center | ~$208,000 | $42,000 | ~$83,600 | Premium family home |

Corrections to the brief fall out of this.

- Model A revenue is not $250,200. It is $169,000 to $208,000 once family-home pricing is applied, using the verified 2024 county median as the floor rather than the unsourced $1,195 figure. The brief's $125,800 provider residual assumed center pricing inside a family home.
- The $42,000 housing-subsidy question the brief flags as decision D4 now has a number. Free rent moves the provider from about $72,000 to about $114,000 take-home at premium pricing, or from about $44,000 to about $86,000 at the corrected county-median floor. That concession is what buys a competitive provider income and the resident licensee the family-home model requires. It is a recruitment cost, not accounting, exactly as the brief argues.
- M1 (below) is answered directionally, not conclusively. Verified county data shows Bothell-area rates already running above the county median before any premium story, and centers already command a 35 to 45 percent premium over FCC medians in the same market, so a well-positioned family home reaching toward the 15 to 25 percent below-center range is not contradicted by county data. But no single verified example of a named 98012 family home charging premium rates was found, so the top of this range is still directional, not confirmed.

## age-mix-capacity-and-demand

Your waitlist note (months-long for infant and toddler rooms) is a demand signal that points the same way as price: infants and toddlers are the highest-tuition and highest-fill seats. But the 3-infant and 4-toddler mix (7 children under age 3) collides with two constraints from the licensing review.

- Family home (Model A), verified against WAC 110-300-0355 and the capacity-waiver rule at WAC 110-300-0358: the under-2 sub-cap is tiered by provider experience and staffing, not a flat 6. A provider under 1 year's experience working alone is capped at 3 children under 2; 1 to 2 years with a qualified assistant allows up to 4 under 2; only the top tier (2-plus years' experience with a qualified assistant, 12-child total capacity) reaches 6 under 2, and only with two walking independently. A 3-infant, 4-toddler mix (up to 7 under age 3, fewer if some toddlers are over 2) is plausible only at the top experience/staffing tier, and needs a qualified assistant either way; it is not automatically non-compliant, but it is not available to a newly licensed provider working alone.
- Center (Model B): infants force a 1:4 ratio (WAC 110-300-0356). Three infants plus four toddlers is at least two staff on the floor before preschool, which is why the brief's single $38,400 assistant line is the model's weakest assumption.

Weighting toward infant and toddler is correct for revenue and fill, but it raises staffing cost in Model B and hits the under-2 cap in Model A. Do not model the premium mix without modeling the staff the ratios force.

The nanny rate you pulled ($26.06 per hour, about $4,448 per month) is the single-family substitute-good ceiling. One child in a center at $2,230 is far below nanny cost, so center pricing has headroom for a single child. A two-child family approaches nanny parity, which is where a sibling discount earns its keep.

## what-decides-4632

- The rent and tuition inputs are sound, so the model's structure is trustworthy. The risk is not invented numbers. It is that the center version has almost no margin and the family-home version earns far less than the brief's table implied.
- Model A (family home) is the more robust fit for 4632 and for GPS's stated real-estate-first strategy. It strips the paid operator salary and center staffing load, the provider's own labor is the residual, and it keeps the RCW 36.70A.450 and RCW 35.21.688 zoning preemption. Its viability hinges on one question: can a home at 4632 command premium family-home rates rather than the $1,195 per month county average? That single question is a larger revenue swing than the rent decision.
- Model B (center) at 4632 needs about 80 percent continuous occupancy to break even and forfeits the zoning preemption that a family home gets automatically. The brief's claim that a 12-child DCYF family license maps to a local "mini-center" bucket under RCW 35.63.170 requiring a conditional use permit does not hold up on verification (see the licensing brief's updated what-decides section): unincorporated Snohomish County's current code (Ordinance 25-015) permits family day-care homes outright with no child-count tier and no mini-center category, and RCW 35.21.688 / RCW 36.70A.450 independently bar a local CUP condition on a home serving 12 or fewer children. A CUP risk only resurfaces if this were a true stand-alone center building over 8,000 sq ft, which a converted single-family home is not. Pursue Model B only to capture the operating margin and only if GPS accepts the employer and compliance exposure, not because of an overstated permitting risk.
- Jurisdiction itself is unresolved: whether 4632 146th Pl SE sits inside Bothell city limits or unincorporated Snohomish County was not conclusively determined from public GIS sources during this verification pass (both jurisdictions' interactive parcel maps are not scriptable). "Bothell, WA 98012" as a postal address does not prove city jurisdiction; the two codes differ in detail even though both permit family day care outright. Confirm via a Snohomish County Assessor/SCOPI parcel-ID lookup before finalizing which municipal code governs.
