# 2026-07-22-washington-childcare-license-and-operating-model-decision-brief

## Purpose and boundary

This is the consolidated record of the Washington discussion for Green Property Solutions (GPS). It is a decision brief, not legal, tax, employment, insurance, lending, fire-code, land-use, or CCR advice. It deliberately does **not** decide whether 4632 146th Pl SE, Bothell, WA 98012 is zoned, code-compliant, insurable, or otherwise approvable. The address is only a calibration property for the financial examples. No conclusion relies on a Zillow listing or an unverified property fact.

The central correction is simple: a resident-operated **family home** and a company-operated **child care center** are different Washington programs. A contract cannot make one behave like the other. GPS must select the operating model before designing its lease, revenue flow, staff relationship, tax treatment, or owner pitch.

## Executive conclusion

For GPS's current strategy, manage homes for third-party owners, place a resident operator, and avoid becoming the child-care business, the independent resident-provider/family-home model is the cleaner default. The resident individual is the licensee, directs care, contracts with parents, receives tuition, and pays actual rent. GPS and the owner earn real-estate and management revenue only.

If GPS wants to collect tuition, direct staffing and enrollment, pay a resident operator, and retain the operating margin, it is choosing a company-operated center. The center licensee must be the operating entity. Each site needs its own approval and capacity. The company accepts payroll, staffing, parent-contract, insurance, and compliance exposure. A center can be located in a private residence only when the child-accessible area is exclusive during care hours or separate from family living quarters. [WAC 110-300-0010](https://app.leg.wa.gov/WAC/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0010).

The proposed arrangement, “the tenant owes $3,500 but receives a $3,500 child-care credit,” does not create $42,000 of owner rent income and a separate $42,000 deductible rent expense. At $0 net rent, it is economically free housing. The accounting must follow the actual transaction; no party should manufacture rent or a tax deduction by paying and refunding the same amount.

## Owner decision tree

Every scenario starts with the **property owner retaining title**. Green Property Solutions does not acquire, hold, or underwrite an ownership position in a property. It works with homes entering the market, turning over, or entering management and asks which owner-approved use produces the highest **annual owner net value per usable square foot**. That value is net of actual owner rent or lease income, vacancy, management fees, conversion and reconversion cost, insurance, maintenance, taxes, debt service where relevant, and owner-borne use costs. A master lease is a leasehold operating arrangement, not ownership.

| Owner decision | Occupant / operator | Who receives rent or tuition | Green Property Solutions role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lease to conventional residential renters | Residential tenant | Owner receives rent. | Property manager, if engaged. |
| Lease to an independent resident family-home provider | Same resident individual is provider/licensee. | Owner receives actual rent; provider receives tuition. | Property manager, conversion coordinator, placement/support provider. It does not operate child care. |
| Lease to a company-operated child care center | Center operating company; a resident director may occupy part of the home. | Owner receives lease rent; operating company receives tuition. | Property manager and, only in this option, the center operating company/licensee/employer. |
| Another owner-approved use | Depends on the selected use. | Depends on the selected use. | Evaluate separately; do not force it into a childcare model. |

The decision tree intentionally contains **no Green Property Solutions ownership branch**. Conventional rental is the control case. Childcare is an owner-elected alternative that must outperform or otherwise strategically justify itself against that control case after actual conversion, vacancy, insurance, management, and reconversion costs. Every owner-choice report should show conventional rental, Model A, Model B, and defined other uses in one table with annual owner net value, annual owner net value per usable square foot, incremental value versus conventional, and the risk that could change the result.

## The non-negotiable fork

| Question | Model A: independent family-home provider | Model B: company-operated center in a residence |
|---|---|---|
| Licensee | Resident individual, operating in that person's family living quarters. | GPS operating company or another legal entity. |
| Residence | The operator lives there. This is definitional. | The resident may be an employee/director but need not be the licensee. |
| Tuition and parent contracts | Provider. | Operating company. |
| Staffing and day-to-day care | Provider. | Operating company. |
| GPS and owner revenue | Actual rent, owner-paid management fees, conversion/support fees. Tuition is only a provider-viability input. | Actual lease rent plus agreed management fees; the operating company also receives tuition and bears center costs. |
| Different resident and operator? | No, if the program is a family home. | Yes, subject to center separation, personnel, background-check, and license requirements. |
| Multiple properties | GPS can support multiple independent providers but is not their family-home licensee. | GPS can operate a network, but each location needs its own DCYF approval, capacity, staffing, and operating file. |
| Main risk | Provider viability and loss of an independent licensee. | Payroll, worker classification, ratios, vacancy, liability, and center compliance. |

Washington defines a family-home program as care for 12 or fewer children in family living quarters where the licensee resides; the licensee is an individual. [WAC 110-300-0005](https://app.leg.wa.gov/WAC/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0005) is the definitions section; the substantive capacity rule, verified 2026-07-23, is [WAC 110-300-0355](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0355), tiered by provider experience and staffing (under 1 year alone: up to 6; 1 to 2 years alone: up to 8; 1 to 2 years with a qualified assistant: up to 9; 2-plus years alone: up to 10, or 12 school-age only; 2-plus years with a qualified assistant: up to 12), plus a capacity-waiver rule at [WAC 110-300-0358](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0358) allowing 13 to 16 children for providers with 3-plus years' experience. The under-2 sub-cap is tiered the same way, reaching 6 (with two walking independently) only at the top tier. DCYF's plain-language guidance says the provider lives in the home. [DCYF provider licensing](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/early-learning-providers/licensed-provider). Do not call a center a family home simply because a worker lives upstairs.

## How the two-story idea works

### Same person lives upstairs and operates downstairs

This can fit either model, but not through the same paperwork. In Model A, the same person should be the whole-home residential tenant and the family-home licensee. Use a residential lease with a child-care-use addendum. Avoid treating that person as a separate commercial tenant of only the downstairs. Their residence and licensed program are connected by the family-home rule.

In Model B, the operating company should lease and control the whole property. It gives the resident employee an upstairs occupancy agreement and uses the downstairs as center space. The resident may be a director/operator, but the company collects tuition and runs the center.

### Person A lives upstairs and Person B operates downstairs

This is not the independent family-home configuration. It is the center route. DCYF may license a center in a private residence when the child-accessible portion is exclusive while children are in care or separate from family living quarters. [WAC 110-300-0010](https://app.leg.wa.gov/WAC/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0010). The actual floor plan, access control, staffing, fire/occupancy approvals, and inspection control, not the lease labels.

A center does not receive an automatic “10-child house” license. Capacity depends on space, age range, equipment, provider history, and available qualified staff. Center ratios include 1:4 for infants, 1:7 for toddlers, and 1:10 for preschoolers, with group-size limits. [WAC 110-300-0356](https://app.leg.wa.gov/WAC/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0356). This brief does not evaluate this building's zoning, CC&Rs, fire clearance, occupancy, parking, accessibility, or local permits. Those are pre-opening gates, not assumptions to bury in the pro forma.

## Contract and entity architecture

### Model A: independent resident provider

The flow is owner/trust to GPS management company, then a residential lease with a child-care-use addendum to the resident individual provider/family-home licensee, then parent contracts, tuition, staffing, licensing, and care operations run directly between provider and families.

The provider must retain genuine operational independence: parent contracts, tuition collection, staffing, attendance, care decisions, records, and licensing accountability. GPS can provide property management, marketing, lead generation, conversion coordination, administrative tools, and owner reporting. If GPS dictates the program as employer-like management while calling the provider independent, obtain counsel on worker classification and on whether GPS is factually operating care.

Rent choices:

1. **Actual market or premium rent.** Provider pays real rent; owner receives rental income; GPS earns its disclosed fee.
2. **True discounted rent.** The lease states the actual lower amount. The owner receives that amount, not a larger headline number.
3. **Free housing.** The lease states $0 rent or other genuine consideration. This is an owner/GPS subsidy, not rent income or a fictional rent expense. GPS needs a separately disclosed owner-paid revenue source if it is to make money.

Avoid the circular arrangement where the provider “pays” $3,500 and GPS/owner immediately refunds or offsets $3,500. It is economically a concession. Do not book it as $42,000 rent income plus a separate $42,000 rent deduction without real payment and specific tax advice.

### Model B: GPS-operated center

The flow is owner/trust to a GPS property-management company under an owner authorization and management agreement, then to the GPS operating company under a whole-premises lease at $3,500/month, then to the center licensee/employer. The operating company holds parent enrollment agreements, tuition, insurance, records, and payroll. It employs the resident director/operator under an employment agreement and an onsite occupancy agreement, and it employs assistant and relief staff.

The owner/trust receives actual lease payments from the operating company. The company receives tuition and pays operator and center costs. The operator can have rent-free upstairs lodging, but it is not a second $42,000 company cash expense if the company already leases the whole home for $42,000. It is a noncash compensation/fringe-benefit question.

Use separate documents, not a blended tenant/operator form:

- owner authorization and property-management agreement, including exact fee, use, insurance, maintenance, and related-party disclosure;
- whole-premises lease between owner/trust and center operating company;
- center-license and director compliance file;
- employee offer, job description, compensation, overtime treatment, and employment policies;
- occupancy agreement tied to employment, with move-out, privacy, access, and emergency terms reviewed for landlord-tenant-law implications;
- parent enrollment agreement and tuition policy held by the center company; and
- related-party disclosure and approval trail where entities share ownership.

Do not call rent-free housing a “rent refund.” If it is part of the employee package, describe it as employer-provided lodging and have payroll/tax counsel decide its wage treatment. The IRS excludes lodging from wages only when it is on the employer's business premises, for the employer's convenience, and required as a condition of employment; a written label alone does not decide it. [IRS Publication 15-B](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p15b).

### Trust ownership

A trust may own the property, but a trustee leasing trust property to themself or an entity they control can be a conflicted transaction. The trustee should sign separately in trustee and individual/entity capacities. Washington trust counsel should review the instrument, beneficiary interests, required consent, valuation, and documentation. Washington's duty-of-loyalty rule can make a conflicted transaction voidable unless an exception or valid approval applies. [RCW 11.98.078](https://app.leg.wa.gov/RCW/default.aspx?cite=11.98.078).

## Wage, tax, and payroll guardrails

- A $50,000 salary is not a solution to an 11-hour, five-day schedule. If the operator is nonexempt, model overtime and all work time. If GPS claims an exemption, actual Washington salary and duties tests must be met; title and free lodging do not decide it.
- Company control over schedule, tuition, parent contracts, staff, and daily care points toward employment. A contractor label does not control the facts.
- A family-home provider who pays actual rent may have a qualifying business-use allocation of actual expenses. A provider paying no rent does not gain a personal rent deduction merely because the home supports child care. [IRS Publication 587](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p587).
- The owner/trust reports actual rental income and deductible expenses. The operating company's actual lease payment may be a business expense if properly structured and used in its trade or business. Employee lodging requires separate wage/fringe review.

## Multi-site rollout

GPS can manage an owner-property portfolio in either model. In Model A, each resident provider holds an individual family-home license and GPS scales support, placement, and property management. In Model B, GPS can operate a multi-site center company, but each location needs site feasibility, its own license application/approval, capacity, approved floor plan, local approvals, staffing plan, insurance review, and director coverage. A corporate licensee does not make a new house automatically operable under another location's license; a center move requires a new address-specific application and inspection. [WAC 110-300-0402](https://app.leg.wa.gov/WAC/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0402). Start with DCYF's current [licensing process](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/early-learning-providers/licensed-provider/licensing-process).

Use four gates per property:

1. Owner elects childcare and signs an explicit use, fee, and conflict disclosure.
2. GPS selects one license model and completes regulatory, insurer, lender, and property feasibility.
3. GPS secures the right resident provider (Model A) or center director, staff, and company-license structure (Model B).
4. Only after licensing and enrollment conditions are met does the property leave conventional-rental inventory.

## Verified licensing and zoning facts (2026-07-23)

Direct primary-source verification of items this brief and its market-anchored companion had left unconfirmed.

- Space requirements: [WAC 110-300-0354](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0354) requires 35 sq ft of indoor licensed program space per child, plus 15 additional sq ft per child under 24 months. [WAC 110-300-0145](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0145) requires 75 sq ft of outdoor play space per child accessing the play space at any given time, with a rotating-group or department-approved off-site alternative. Both apply to family homes and centers alike.
- Provider education requirement: [WAC 110-300-0100](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0100) requires a high school diploma or equivalent at application, an ECE initial certificate or equivalent within five years of licensure (or by August 1, 2030, whichever is later), and an ECE short certificate or equivalent within two years after that. The requirement is uniform across family-home capacity tiers; there is no separate, higher ECE-credit threshold for a higher-capacity license.
- License fees: [WAC 110-300-0401](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=110-300-0401) sets the family-home annual fee at a flat $30, nonrefundable, due at initial application and annually 30 days before the license anniversary (centers pay $125 for the first 12 children plus $12 per additional child). Not tiered by family-home capacity.
- Zoning preemption text, confirmed directly from app.leg.wa.gov: RCW 36.70A.450 bars a county from prohibiting a residential dwelling's use as a family day-care provider's home facility in a residentially or commercially zoned area, though it may condition lot size, setbacks, hours, or a loading area. RCW 35.21.688 is the parallel city/town provision, explicitly scoped to homes serving 12 or fewer children. RCW 35.63.185 is a near-identical general-city provision. All three define "family day-care provider" by reference to the current DCYF statute, RCW 43.216.010.
- Unincorporated Snohomish County code, confirmed against current Ordinance 25-015 (adopted 2026-03-19, effective 2026-03-30): "Family Day Care Home" is an outright permitted use in every listed urban and rural residential zone, with no child-count tier and no conditional-use requirement. The county's use matrix has no separate "mini-day care center" category; a non-family "Day Care Center" use is permitted up to 8,000 sq ft and conditional only above that size, which does not describe a converted single-family home.
- City of Bothell municipal code: search-snippet evidence (BMC 12.06.140) indicates family day care for 12 or fewer children is a permitted residential use consistent with the state preemption, but the snippet cites the pre-2018 licensing statute (former RCW 74.15.020), which suggests the cited section may be stale. This was not independently confirmed against the live Bothell municipal code site, which blocked direct fetch; confirm directly with the city before relying on it.
- Jurisdiction of 4632 146th Pl SE: not conclusively determined. "Bothell, WA 98012" is a postal address and does not by itself prove city-limits jurisdiction; both Bothell and unincorporated Snohomish County GIS parcel lookups were not resolvable via web search during this pass. Confirm via the Snohomish County Assessor or SCOPI parcel-ID lookup before deciding which municipal code (BMC vs. SCC) governs, since that determines the permitting desk even though both jurisdictions permit family day care outright.
- Correction to the market-anchored companion doc's claim that a 12-child DCYF family license maps to a "mini-day care center" bucket under RCW 35.63.170 requiring a conditional use permit: RCW 35.63.170 does define a mini-day care center to include 7 to 12 children in the family abode, so the definitional premise is accurate, but the CUP-risk conclusion does not hold up. Unincorporated Snohomish County's current code does not use that tier and permits family day-care homes outright regardless of count, and RCW 35.21.688 / RCW 36.70A.450 independently bar a local CUP condition on a home serving 12 or fewer children. RCW 35A.63.210, which required code cities to review mini/day-care-center CUP costs, was a one-time 1989 to 1991 compliance mandate pushing cities to eliminate such CUP requirements, not an ongoing authorization to impose one. A CUP risk would only resurface for a true stand-alone center building over 8,000 sq ft, which a converted single-family home is not.

## LLM and counsel adversarial prompts

1. **Classification attack:** “Assume GPS calls this Model A but sets hours, tuition, enrollment, curriculum, staffing, and parent contracts. Does the factual model instead show GPS operating care? Identify the contract and behavior changes required.”
2. **Residency attack:** “Can one person live upstairs while another person operates downstairs and still support a Washington family-home license? Quote the controlling definition and identify the correct alternative.”
3. **Double-counting audit:** “Trace every dollar of the $3,500/month. Is it owner income, OpCo expense, employee lodging value, provider expense, or a noncash concession? Flag duplicates.”
4. **Labor attack:** “Rebuild the 7:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m. schedule by age mix and WAC ratios. Add opening/closing, breaks, absences, overtime, administration, and relief. Does the $38,400 staffing placeholder survive?”
5. **Downside test:** “Run 80%, 85%, 90%, and 100% enrollment, 10% lower tuition, and a two-month launch delay. At what operator salary does the center turn cash-negative before debt service?”
6. **Trust attack:** “Assume a trustee or affiliate is on both landlord and tenant/operator sides. Identify the consent, disclosure, valuation, and approval trail required to mitigate self-dealing risk.”
7. **Owner-choice recommendation:** “Start with an owner property entering the market, turning over, or entering management, with the owner retaining title. Compare conventional renters, Model A, Model B, and any other owner-approved use. For each, calculate annual owner net value and annual owner net value per usable square foot after all owner costs. State which choice wins, why, and what unknown could reverse the recommendation. Reject any analysis that assumes Green Property Solutions owns the property or that treats gross rent or tuition as owner value.”
