# 2026-07-21-conversion-analysis-continuation-prompt

You are building a decision-grade financial analysis for Green-Lappe Properties, a residential rental business with two parts: a property management company, and a real-estate platform that acquires or controls residences, converts them into licensed early childhood education facilities for children under 5, and places licensed providers into them. Green-Lappe and the property owners never operate childcare. The provider is the tenant. Green-Lappe's revenue is real estate revenue only: rent, rent premium over conventional residential, property management fees, and recovery of conversion capital through the lease. Childcare tuition is never Green-Lappe revenue; it enters the analysis only as the provider-viability check that caps the rent a provider can sustainably pay. Any draft that books tuition to Green-Lappe is wrong; stop and restructure.

The decision, per property and per market: (A) conventional residential rental under property management, versus (B1) conversion to a family-home childcare facility leased to a provider who resides in the home, versus (B2) conversion to a small center or preschool leased to a non-resident operator. The live-versus-operate fork is regulatory, not preferential: family-home licenses in both states require the licensee to reside in the home, so B1 is a live-and-operate tenancy by law, and B2 requires a center license with change-of-occupancy consequences. Where B2 is infeasible for a property class or jurisdiction, say so and collapse to B1. Markets: Orange, San Diego, Riverside, and Los Angeles counties in California; Snohomish and King counties in Washington. Evaluate each county individually and blended. If an option does not work, the first line of the verdict says so and quantifies why.

## PHASES, IN ORDER
Do not begin the workbook until phase 13 verification passes against an independent computation.

**Phase 0, acceptance criteria before modeling.** State thresholds before results exist: minimum DSCR, maximum capital to terminal EBITDA, maximum ruin probability, minimum cash-on-cash and return on invested capital versus the conventional-rental baseline, payback ceiling on conversion capex, and maximum acceptable vacancy-between-providers. Option A is the baseline: B1 and B2 are graded on their spread over A, not in isolation.

**Phase 1, ground-truth teardown before modeling.** What does a provider's alternative cost: renting any ordinary house and licensing it themselves, which both states allow, so name precisely what Green-Lappe sells that a provider cannot get from an ordinary landlord (licensable-ready condition, landlord consent certainty, conversion capital they do not have, tolerance of the use, speed to license). What does a landlord's alternative earn: conventional rent with none of the conversion capex or specialized vacancy risk. Establish current licensed-capacity shortfall by county (child care desert ratios), family child care home closure and exit rates over 3 years, and the failure history of childcare real estate platforms. Name the single unvalidated assumption each option lives or dies on and label it everywhere it appears; for B1 expect it to be the achievable rent premium over conventional rent, and for B2 the entitlement timeline.

**Phase 2, regulatory and licensing map with exact citations, per state and county.** California: family child care homes under HSC 1597.30 et seq., small up to 8 and large up to 14, the requirement that the home be the licensee's residence, SB 234 treatment of large family child care as residential use statewide with zoning preemption, tenant rights to operate family child care in rentals and what notice a landlord is owed, fire clearance and pool fencing, and indoor and outdoor square footage per child. Center licensing under Title 22 for B2: change of occupancy to E or I-4, conditional use permits by county and city, ADA, fire, and parking. Washington: DCYF licensing under WAC 110-300, the RCW 43.216 definition confining family home care to the provider's residence, capacity tiers, King and Snohomish county and city zoning treatment of home-based care and small centers, and HB 1199 limits on HOA prohibitions. For both states: what a landlord may and may not condition in a lease to a provider, insurance obligations each party can be required to carry, and licensing timelines in months as dead time before rent. Exemptions are scope, never invented; if none exists, say none exists. Flag anything unverifiable as unconfirmed.

**Phase 3, cost inputs with mean and median.** Conversion capex itemized per property class for B1 (egress, fencing, bathroom count, outdoor surface, fire and safety, lead and pool remediation) and B2 (occupancy change, sprinklers where triggered, ADA, parking), each with mean and median from sourced figures. Reconversion cost to return to conventional rental, which is the exit event both B options must carry. Recurring: landlord insurance surcharge for licensed childcare use with mean and median quotes, maintenance delta over conventional tenancy from 12 to 14 children of daily wear, property tax reassessment consequences of conversion in each state, vacancy-between-providers in months. Provider-side (for the viability check only): achievable tuition by county, subsidy ceilings by county from the California regional market rate and Washington Working Connections rates, provider staffing costs at licensed ratios. Search everything current; never rely on memory for a price, rate, or subsidy.

**Phase 4, labor accounting.** Green-Lappe's hours are landlord and property management hours in every option; there are no operator hours because Green-Lappe never operates. Price the management delta: hours per property per month for A versus B1 versus B2, including provider sourcing, licensing support, and inspections liaison, at a loaded wage and at zero. Separately, run the provider's labor reality inside the viability check: a family-home provider works 50 to 60 hours per week, and the rent Green-Lappe charges must leave the provider an income above their wage alternative or the tenancy churns. Compute the provider take-home at capacity and at realistic enrollment, and state the effective hourly wage the rent structure leaves them, because that number, not the lease, determines renewal.

**Phase 5, taxes on both sides of every transaction.** Green-Lappe side: residential rental income and depreciation (27.5-year residential versus what conversion does to classification for B2), conversion capex treatment (improvement capitalization versus repairs, cost segregation, current IRC 179 and 168(k) status searched not remembered, and the fact that residential rental buildings themselves never qualify for bonus), Washington's absence of income tax against its B&O tax treatment of rental income and property management fees, California state treatment, and property tax reassessment on conversion. Transaction taxes: Washington B&O on management fees, any sales tax exposure on services, and local business license taxes by city. Provider side inside the viability check: the provider's tax position only as it affects sustainable rent. Tax-exempt structures if any owner is one. If a treatment cannot change the A-versus-B ranking, state the invariance and move on.

**Phase 6, NPV per stakeholder at stakeholder-specific discount rates.** One tab per stakeholder: the property owner (investment-property mortgage or HELOC rates, searched current), Green-Lappe as manager-converter (its cost of capital and opportunity cost of hours), and the provider-tenant (their viability at the proposed rent, discounted at a shadow rate reflecting no access to cheap capital). Ten-year horizon per property: A as baseline, B1 and B2 with conversion capex in year 0, licensing dead months, rent premium, provider-turnover vacancy events at the phase-3 frequency, reconversion cost at exit, terminal value under both continue-as-childcare and revert-to-rental. Mean-cost and median-cost variants, labor priced and at zero. The binding constraint each way: B rent is capped from below by provider viability and from above by owner return; if the corridor between those two numbers is empty in a county, the model must say the corridor is empty.

**Phase 7, market-level margin.** Only the lines geography touches may vary: conventional market rent, achievable childcare rent premium, tuition and subsidy ceilings, conversion cost indices, licensing timeline, and vacancy. Hold all else identical so differences are attributable. Report per-property margin and the corridor width from phase 6 by county, and state which county wins on spread over conventional rental versus which wins on absolute dollars, because they will differ. Expect the subsidy-rate structure to make some lower-rent counties better B1 markets than higher-rent ones; test that expectation explicitly.

**Phase 8, TAM, SAM, SOM with definitions.** TAM: annual rent value of residences suitable for licensed childcare conversion across the six counties, defined in one sentence before computing, built from the licensed-capacity shortfall translated into needed facilities times achievable rent. SAM: the slice reachable by Green-Lappe's model, gated by the count of properties matching the acquisition box and the supply of licensable providers, whichever binds first, and say which binds. SOM: the output of the phase-11 operating model only, reported as a share of SAM and flagged above 25 percent. The provider supply question is first-class: converting homes is pointless if licensed providers are the scarce input, so size the provider pipeline by county from licensing issuance data.

**Phase 9, competition and incumbent response.** Census by name: childcare real estate investors and franchisors, center chains acquiring residential parcels, Wonderschool-style platform models, and ordinary landlords who simply tolerate family child care, which SB 234 and Washington law force them to do, making every landlord a latent competitor at zero premium. Model the price-compression scenario: if tolerant ordinary landlords cap the premium at 10 percent, what survives. State Green-Lappe's structural advantage if one exists (conversion capital, licensing navigation, provider pipeline) and if none exists, say none exists.

**Phase 10, demand validation plan with decision rules.** Two assumptions gate everything: the rent premium a provider will pay, and provider supply. Design the cheapest tests: list one converted or convert-ready property and measure provider applications and accepted rent within N weeks; run a provider-intent campaign through county resource-and-referral agencies and family child care associations with a target of X letters of intent at the modeled rent. Attach decision rules of the form: fewer than X applications at the modeled premium within N weeks disproves the central case and stops acquisition. Identify the exogenous triggers that move demand discontinuously, universal pre-K expansion in either state, subsidy rate changes, Working Connections eligibility shifts, and model each as a scenario branch with its leading indicator.

**Phase 11, phased operating model with debt at current rates.** Monthly engine over 60 months across the six counties with entry gates, per-property pipeline stages (acquire, convert, license dead months, place provider, stabilize), conversion capex drawn on debt at searched current investment-property and renovation rates with proper amortization, provider-turnover events with re-placement vacancy, working capital including deposits and rent-commencement lag, property management fee revenue on both the A book and B book, staffing steps for Green-Lappe at loaded wages, insurance, and the acquisition funnel with cost per placed provider. Include at least one channel scenario: a master agreement with a provider network, resource-and-referral agency, or employer-sponsored childcare buyer that delivers multiple placements per agreement, priced against one-by-one placement. Include rent architecture: flat premium versus base-plus-percentage versus capex-amortization rider, tested against provider viability. Output cash trough, month, equity required with an operating floor, DSCR at month 60, and capital to terminal EBITDA.

**Phase 12, risk quantification.** Probabilities across the scenario space and expected value, ruin probability against committed equity, tornado ranked by effect on 60-month cash including the two-way interaction of rent premium crossed with provider-turnover rate. Tail risks with cites: licensing revocation or abuse allegation at a property and its vacancy and reputational consequences, statute amendment in either direction, insurance market withdrawal from childcare-use dwellings, and the landlord's liability exposure for a tenant-operated facility including what standard landlord policies exclude.

**Phase 13, terminal value and exit, then verification.** Value the month-60 platform under build-to-hold and build-to-sell: stabilized childcare-tenanted homes at cap rates versus conventional rentals, the management book at recurring-revenue multiples, named plausible acquirers among childcare real estate funds and regional operators, and the verdict restated including terminal value. Verification is mandatory: compute the full model in an independent scripted calculation first, print reference values, build the workbook second, recalculate headlessly to zero formula errors, and tie every NPV, corridor, and verdict cell to the reference to the dollar, printing the comparison. Known failure modes: sheet names containing hyphens must be single-quoted in cross-sheet formulas; never place an input in a row a later header write will overwrite; when adding rows, re-audit every hardcoded cell anchor referencing that sheet.
