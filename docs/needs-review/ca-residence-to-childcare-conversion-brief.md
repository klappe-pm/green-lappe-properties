# ca-residence-to-childcare-conversion-brief

This brief ports the Washington family-child-care conversion model to California: the laws, the license types, and the mechanics of making a residential property available for licensed child care. It is regulatory research, not legal, tax, insurance, zoning, or lease advice. Before any lease or construction commitment, obtain a written feasibility review from the local CDSS regional office, the local planning and building authority, and the fire authority, plus California counsel and insurance advice.

## What is being ported

The Washington model that the six-county analysis validated is the conversion wedge: Green-Lappe does not operate care and does not buy the building. A property owner keeps the property, a licensed provider resides in the home and operates the child care business, and Green-Lappe coordinates the conversion, places the provider, and runs ongoing services for fees. The owner earns premium rent; the provider owns the care operation and the license. The whole California question is whether that same structure is legal and licensable in California, and what physically and legally has to change to make a residence a care facility.

The short answer: the structure ports cleanly. California, like Washington, licenses in-home care through the resident provider, voids most residential restrictions that would block a family child care home, and lets a corporate entity be the applicant only on the separate child care center route. The differences are in the numbers and the agency: California is CDSS and Title 22, Washington is DCYF; California caps a large home at 12 (14 conditional), Washington at 12; California requires a one-year experience gate for the large home that Washington structures differently.

## The four California models

A platform or a coordinator can supply marketing, software, coaching, and payment tools, but none of that replaces California licensing. The model turns on who provides or manages care, whether the operator resides in the property, whether care is paid, capacity, and staffing.

| Model | Who holds the license | Operator resides there | Paid care | Capacity and staffing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small FCCH | Resident provider applies to CDSS | Yes, care is in the provider's own residence | Allowed | Up to 6 children, or up to 8 only with the statutory age mix, infant limit, parent notification, and renter-consent conditions. Resident children under 10 count. |
| Large FCCH | Resident provider applies to CDSS | Yes | Allowed | Up to 12 only while an assistant provider is in the home, no more than 4 infants. Up to 14 requires specific school-age and infant conditions, parent notification, and written owner consent if rented. Without the assistant, capacity reverts to Small FCCH rules. |
| Unpaid parent cooperative | No license under a narrow exemption if every condition holds | No FCCH residence rule, but other property rules may apply | No payment or in-kind income for care | Parents rotate as caregiver, each related to at least one child, no more than 12 at once. Not an income model. |
| Licensed child care center or preschool | An individual, partnership, corporation, LLC, or other entity legally responsible for the facility | No, this is a non-residential group setting | Allowed | Set by the center license and Title 22 ratios. Requires approved fire clearance and a qualified director on site. |

Three corrections to the common platform pitch, confirmed against the California code. A large home ordinarily serves up to 12 with an assistant present, not 14 as a routine number. A ninth child requires an assistant physically in the home; without one, the home must comply with the eight-child small-home limit, and a platform cannot substitute for that person. Paid pooled care is not the unpaid parent-cooperative exemption; a fee, a paid host, or a regular non-parent caregiver breaks it.

## The three rental-property structures

### Structure 1: landlord to a resident FCCH provider

This is the cleaner residential model and the direct port of the Washington wedge. The tenant must genuinely reside in the rental, because a California FCCH is care in the provider's own home. A provider who rents must give the owner 30 days' written notice before opening (HSC 1597.41). California generally voids residential-property restrictions that prohibit a family day care home, while the owner keeps ordinary rights over noise, nuisance, lease violations, and may require an increased security deposit within the statutory cap. For a 13 to 14 child large home, the resident operator needs written owner consent. The operator, not the landlord, is the CDSS applicant and licensee, holds the parent contracts, controls staffing and attendance, keeps the records, and carries the required insurance or the parent-affidavit alternative. The lease should explicitly allocate maintenance, indemnity, insurance, parking, outdoor space, alterations, incident reporting, and the consequence of license suspension or loss. That allocation is a commercial risk decision; it does not shift the license holder's statutory responsibility.

### Structure 2: landlord to a separately licensed center or preschool

If care will run where no provider resides, it is a child care center, not an FCCH. The center applicant can be a company; CDSS instructions contemplate corporate, partnership, and LLC applicants and request the facility address and control-of-property information. Unlike an FCCH, a center must secure and maintain approved fire clearance, and must have a qualified director on premises while open. The owner need not personally meet director qualifications if it is only the landlord, or if the licensee entity hires a qualified director. Before advertising or signing a long-term lease, get written feasibility from the CDSS regional office, planning and building, and fire, because fire clearance, occupancy, access, outdoor space, parking, accessibility, local building standards, lender restrictions, and land-use conditions can decide the project.

### Structure 3: owner as developer, not operator

Green-Lappe creates value by identifying a suitable property, documenting feasibility, financing permissible improvements, and leasing to an experienced licensee. Keep a real boundary: the tenant or licensee directs care, hires and supervises staff, manages enrollment and tuition, keeps child records, controls capacity, and owns compliance. The moment the owner begins establishing, managing, conducting, or maintaining the child care business, it should assume it may be treated as the operator and use the licensed-center route with the compliance infrastructure that entails. This is the load-bearing line for the Green-Lappe model: coordinator and developer, never operator of the care.

## Converting a residence to a care facility

The physical and legal conversion depends on which license type. The FCCH route is light; the center route is heavy. This is the practical port of the Washington conversion capex ($15,000 to $40,000 full, $3,000 to $10,000 light) into California requirements.

### FCCH conversion (Structures 1 and 3, resident provider)

- Zoning and land use: a California family day care home is treated as a residential use of property by law, so most local zoning, occupancy, and business-license barriers that would block it are void. This is the California parallel to Washington's RCW 35.63.185 by-right preemption. HOA CC&Rs and private lease terms remain the binding constraint to check per property.
- Physical requirements: no fire-clearance inspection of the same kind a center needs, but the home must meet FCCH health and safety standards. Yard fencing is not mandated for a California FCCH, but pool and spa fencing is where water is present. Smoke and carbon monoxide detectors, safe storage, and egress are checked.
- Licensing: the resident provider applies to CDSS, completes the orientation, background clearance for everyone in the home, required training (pediatric CPR and first aid, preventive health, and mandated-reporter awareness), and passes the pre-license inspection. Budget a licensing lead time on the order of a few months, confirmed with the regional office.
- Capacity gate: to run a large home the resident provider needs one year of regulated small-home operator or licensed-center administrator experience, unless CDSS waives it for sufficient qualifying experience. This gate is why the ramp analysis starts a new provider at a small-home capacity and steps up.

### Center conversion (Structure 2, non-resident operator)

- Fire clearance is mandatory and is the usual gating item: a residential structure used as a center must pass fire-authority clearance for the group occupancy, which can trigger sprinklers, alarms, exits, and accessibility work.
- Building and occupancy: the change from residential to a group care occupancy raises building-code, ADA access, parking, and outdoor-play requirements that a house may not meet without significant work.
- Director and staffing: a qualified director must be on site while open, with a qualified substitute during absence, and Title 22 ratios set staffing.
- Because of these, the center route is only worth it where the demand and rate structure support the heavier capex and the operator is an experienced licensee, not the owner.

## California versus Washington, side by side

| Dimension | California | Washington |
|---|---|---|
| Agency and rules | CDSS, Title 22 (22 CCR) | DCYF, WAC child care licensing |
| In-home license | Small FCCH up to 8, Large FCCH up to 12 (14 conditional) with assistant | Licensed Family Home up to 12, provider resides |
| Zoning protection | Family day care home is a residential use by statute; local bans void | RCW 35.63.185 preempts city restriction, by-right |
| Large-home experience gate | One year small-home or center-admin experience, or CDSS waiver | Structured through DCYF qualifications and staffing |
| Renter provider consent | 30-day written notice to owner (HSC 1597.41); written owner consent for 13 to 14 | Standard owner consent; state law voids blanket bans |
| Center applicant | Individual, partnership, corporation, LLC, public agency | Entity applicant on the center route |
| Fire clearance | Not FCCH; required for a center | Not family home; required for a center |
| Real-estate brokerage layer for third-party PM | CA DRE, B&P 10130 and 10131 | RCW 18.85, designated broker regime |
| Subsidy rate reference | CDSS Regional Market Rate, confirmed frozen at January 2022 | DCYF base rate, rises toward the 75th percentile July 2027 |

The economic consequence of the last two rows is the whole reason California and Washington diverge in the six-county analysis: California tuition is pinned near a subsidy ceiling frozen since January 2022, so at market pricing a provider cannot outbid a family tenant, while Washington's higher and rising rate lets the provider pay well above market rent. The regulatory structures port; the pricing does not.

## Diligence checklist before any California commitment

- Ask the CDSS regional office to classify the proposed model in writing: resident FCCH, child care center, or a claimed exemption.
- Obtain parcel-specific land-use, building, and fire feasibility before any lease or construction.
- Decide explicitly whether Green-Lappe is landlord and developer only, or the center applicant, then align the entity, lease, insurance, staffing, and parent contracts to that choice. Do not blur the line.
- For an FCCH, confirm the licensee resides there, count resident children under 10, determine small versus large capacity, and obtain the owner notice or the written consent the planned capacity requires.
- For a center, identify the applicant entity and the qualified site director before filing; do not rely on an owner without director qualifications to lead the program.
- Obtain California counsel and insurance advice on lease, general liability, professional liability, abuse and molestation coverage, additional-insured status, lender consent, and local business and tax issues.
- Run the tuition survey first. The California go decision rests on whether a provider can collect roughly $1,500 to $2,000 per child per month in the target ZIPs, which no regulatory clearance can establish.
