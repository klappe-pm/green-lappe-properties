# 2026-07-21-conversion-analysis-prompt-v2

You are building a decision-grade, owner-side highest-and-best-use analysis for Green Property Solutions (GPS). GPS works with third-party owners whose properties are entering the market, turning over, or entering its management pipeline. GPS does not acquire, own, or pay for the real estate. The owner retains title in every California and Washington scenario. The product is not childcare real estate by default. It is a repeatable decision that finds the most valuable permitted and feasible use of each home's square footage.

The primary owner metric is **annual owner net value per usable square foot**, with annual owner net value and incremental value versus a conventional rental shown alongside it. Calculate owner net value only after actual rent or lease income, vacancy, management fees, conversion and reconversion costs, insurance, maintenance, property taxes, debt service where relevant, and use-specific costs allocated to the owner. Gross rent, gross tuition, and a headline rent credit are never substitutes for this metric. Rank alternatives by the owner result, then state the operational, regulatory, and demand risk that could reverse the ranking.

Control comes through two owner-title-preserving supply channels, graded separately through every phase and never blended silently: (C1) an owner-management or conversion mandate, the primary channel, and (C2) a master lease from an owner to GPS or a GPS operating company. A master lease is a leasehold arrangement, not ownership, and imports lease-spread risk and, in Washington, potential B&O exposure. GPS may earn disclosed management, conversion-coordination, placement, or operating-company fees. It must not invent property ownership, acquired-property depreciation, or property-acquisition capital in the analysis.

The decision, per property and per market, is a complete owner choice set: (A) conventional residential rental; (B1) an independent family-home childcare provider who resides in the home and receives tuition; (B2) a company-operated center or preschool, which may collect tuition only if the GPS operating company is the actual center licensee and employer; and (C) another owner-approved use, defined and modeled when identified. Do not presume childcare wins. The live-versus-operate fork is regulatory, not preferential: family-home licenses in both states require the licensee to reside in the home, so B1 is a live-and-operate tenancy by law, and B2 requires a center license with change-of-occupancy consequences. Two verified priors on B2 to re-test, not rediscover: a non-resident operator makes the rent commercial, and past 20% commercial gross rent the owner's building flips from 27.5-year residential to 39-year depreciation under the IRC 168(e)(2)(A) 80% dwelling-unit test, a structural tax penalty B1 avoids by construction; and at modeled prices B2-scale staffing at licensed ratios already produced negative NPV in both Washington counties (a 28-child configuration with four teachers plus director loses money; the two-staff family-home configurations were the profit frontier). Where B2 is infeasible for a property class or jurisdiction, say so. Markets: Orange, San Diego, Riverside, and Los Angeles counties in California; Snohomish and King counties in Washington. Model costs and rents by county and then by target city within each county; county averages hide the corridor. Evaluate each county individually and blended. If an option does not work, the first line of the verdict says so and quantifies why.

## PHASES, IN ORDER

Do not begin the workbook until phase 13 verification passes against an independent computation.

**Phase 0, acceptance criteria before modeling.** State thresholds before results exist. Defaults to accept or override explicitly: minimum DSCR 1.25; maximum capital to terminal EBITDA 5x; maximum ruin probability 5% of committed equity; conversion capex payback ceiling 24 months; maximum vacancy-between-providers 4 months; minimum spread of B over A of 300 basis points on cash-on-cash and on return on invested capital. Option A is the owner’s conventional-renter baseline. B1, B2, and any defined C option are graded on their incremental annual owner net value and annual owner net value per usable square foot over A, not in isolation. The model must reject any scenario in which GPS acquires or owns the property.

**Phase 1, ground-truth teardown before modeling.** Start with the owner, not childcare: establish the conventional-rental control case and the value of every proposed alternative on both a total-dollar and usable-square-foot basis. What does a provider's alternative cost: renting any ordinary house and licensing it themselves, which both states allow, so name precisely what GPS sells that a provider cannot get from an ordinary landlord (licensable-ready condition, landlord consent certainty, conversion capital they do not have, tolerance of the use, speed to license). What does an owner give up and gain relative to conventional rent, including specialized vacancy and reconversion risk? Establish current licensed-capacity shortfall by county from the settled dataset below, updated only where stale. Provider churn priors, no direct 1-to-3-year FCCH closure statistic exists anywhere: use Minnesota licensing churn 12-14% annually and the Florida survival study (19.7% year-1, 50.6% year-5 business failure; subsidy participation extends survival) as labeled proxies. Platform failure history is settled: MyVillage dead, NeighborSchools absorbed then bankrupted with Higher Ground (Ch.11 June 2025, $440M losses), Mission Driven Finance's CARE fund survives only on concessionary philanthropy capital; services-only revenue did not cover CAC in any of them. Name the single unvalidated assumption each option lives or dies on and label it everywhere it appears; for B1 expect it to be the achievable rent premium over conventional rent, and for B2 the entitlement timeline.

**Phase 2, regulatory and licensing map with exact citations, per state and county.** Most of this is verified in the settled dataset; re-cite, spot-check anything older than this file, and extend to city zoning only. California: HSC 1597.30 et seq.; small FCCH 4 solo / 6 (max 3 infants) / 7-8 conditional (HSC 1597.44); large 12 (max 4 infants) / 13-14 conditional (HSC 1597.465); hard cap 14; assistant may be 14+; residence required (HSC 1596.78); SB 234 makes small and large FCCH by-right residential use statewide (HSC 1597.45), no CUP, no business-license fee; tenant right to operate with 30-day written notice to the landlord (HSC 1597.41); pool fencing required (22 CCR 102417(g)(5)); yard fencing NOT required (fenced or supervised, 102417(g)(6)); NO indoor or outdoor square-footage-per-child requirement for FCCH (the 75 sqft rule is centers-only); do not model invented space standards. Center licensing for B2: Title 22 ratios (infant 1:4, toddler option 1:6, preschool 1:12, 1:15 with aide, 1:18 fully-qualified-plus-aide), director on premises, fire clearance, E-occupancy change, local CUP typical, ADA, parking. Washington: WAC 110-300-0355 capacity tied to licensee experience (solo 6; 1-2 yrs 8-9; 2+ yrs with assistant 12, max 6 under-2); waiver 13-16 (WAC 110-300-0358: 3+ yrs experience, ECE certs, 35 sqft indoor per child plus 15 for under-24-months, 75 sqft outdoor or rotation plan, sprinkler generally required); residence required (RCW 43.216.010); center-in-residence expressly allowed (WAC 110-300-0010(4)); license attaches to the facility, provider credentials portable, 90-day decision on complete application (RCW 43.216.305); HB 1199 limits HOA prohibitions. For both states: what a landlord may and may not condition in a lease to a provider, insurance each party can be required to carry (CA FCCH floor: $100K/$300K liability or bond or parent affidavits, HSC 1597.531), and licensing timelines as dead months before rent (CA realistic 3-5 months; WA 90-day statutory decision plus practical lead). Exemptions are scope, never invented; if none exists, say none exists. Flag anything unverifiable as unconfirmed.

**Phase 3, cost inputs with mean and median, by county then city.** Conversion capex itemized per property class for B1 (egress, fencing where elected, bathroom count, outdoor surface, fire and safety, lead and pool remediation) and B2 (occupancy change, sprinklers where triggered, ADA, parking), each with mean and median from sourced figures; settled anchors: B1 mean $22,000 / median $15,000 with component ranges on file; reconversion $9,000-12,000 as the exit event both B options carry. Recurring: landlord insurance surcharge for licensed childcare use (settled: care liability $800-2,000/yr layered on landlord policy; verify quotes), maintenance delta over conventional tenancy at 12-14 children of daily wear (baseline SoCal SFH $1,000-2,500/unit/yr, care multiplier ~1.5x), vacancy-between-providers in months. Property tax reassessment is answered, state it as fact: California use alone never reassesses, major conversion work can be assessed as new construction for the altered portion only (R&T 70(a)), Prop 13 base otherwise preserved; Washington has no use-based reclassification. Provider-side for the viability check only: achievable tuition by county AND by target city (city-level is required and is the main new research in this phase), subsidy ceilings from the settled dataset (CA RMR frozen at 1/1/2022 levels; WA DCYF July 2025 base rates with centers at the 85th percentile from July 2026 and family homes scheduled to the 75th July 2027), provider staffing at licensed ratios and market wages. Search anything time-sensitive; never rely on memory for a price, rate, or subsidy.

**Phase 4, labor accounting.** GPS's hours are owner-management and conversion-coordination hours in A and B1; there are no GPS care-operator hours in the independent-provider model. In B2, separately price the center operating company's director, teacher, relief, payroll-tax, benefit, and administration hours. Do not hide them in a rent premium. Price the management delta: hours per property per month for A versus B1 versus B2, including provider sourcing, licensing support, and inspections liaison, at a loaded wage and at zero; settled anchor: ~120 hours per conversion plus ~3 hours per week per active converted door. Separately, run the provider's labor reality inside the B1 viability check: a family-home provider works 50 to 60 hours per week, and the rent must leave the provider an income above their wage alternative (childcare market wage $19.40-19.82/hr CA, ~$21 WA) or the tenancy churns. Compute provider take-home at capacity and at realistic enrollment (85-90% by slot-gap severity), include the provider's IRC 280A(c)(4) time-space deduction (30-40% of rent and utilities deductible for a full-time provider, roughly +13% rent capacity; renters qualify per Pub 587), and state the effective hourly wage the rent structure leaves them, because that number, not the lease, determines renewal.

**Phase 5, taxes on both sides of every transaction.** Most of this chain is verified in the settled dataset; apply it, extend only where the channel changes it. Green Property Solutions receives C1 service income and may receive C2 leasehold operating income. The property owner remains the owner-side taxpayer in every scenario. Analyze the owner’s rental, depreciation, conversion-capex, and local tax treatment separately from Green Property Solutions’ management or operating-company tax treatment. Do not introduce an acquisition, owned-property depreciation, or ownership-position scenario for Green Property Solutions. Provider-side tax treatment matters only as it affects sustainable rent. If a treatment cannot change the conventional-renter versus childcare ranking, state the invariance and move on.

**Phase 6, NPV per stakeholder at stakeholder-specific discount rates.** One tab per stakeholder: the property owner (HELOC 7.43% and investment-property rates searched current), Green Property Solutions as manager-converter (equity hurdle 10-12%, opportunity cost of hours), and the provider-tenant (shadow rate reflecting no access to cheap capital). Ten-year horizon per property: A as baseline, B1 and B2 with conversion capex in year 0 allocated per the cost-ownership map (owner absorbs conversion, Green Property Solutions absorbs licensing, fronted variable costs pass through at a 10% margin on top of the management fee; management fee grid 5, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12 percent), licensing dead months, rent premium, provider-turnover vacancy events at the phase-3 frequency, reconversion cost at exit, terminal value under both continue-as-childcare and revert-to-rental. Mean-cost and median-cost variants, labor priced and at zero. The binding constraint each way: B rent is capped from below by provider viability and from above by owner return; if the corridor between those two numbers is empty in a county or city, the model must say the corridor is empty. Settled corridor evidence to reconcile against, not re-derive: at market tuition the provider ceiling clears conventional rent decisively in King and Snohomish (premium roughly $4,500-6,000/month with the tax uplift), thinly in Orange, and fails in San Diego, Riverside, and Los Angeles at prevailing tuition, with the caveat that prevailing CA tuition data is 2019-2022 vintage escalated and city-level rates are unmeasured, which is exactly what phase 3 city work resolves.

**Phase 7, market-level margin.** Only the lines geography touches may vary: conventional market rent, achievable childcare rent premium, tuition and subsidy ceilings by county and city, conversion cost indices, licensing timeline, and vacancy. Hold all else identical so differences are attributable. Report annual owner net value, annual owner net value per usable square foot, incremental value over conventional rental, per-property margin, and corridor width by county and target city. State which market wins on owner productivity versus which wins on absolute dollars, because they may differ. Test explicitly whether subsidy-rate structure makes some lower-rent markets better B1 markets than higher-rent ones, and fold in the demand trajectory: CA under-5 population is projected down 2.5-8.4% by 2030 by county while King and Snohomish grow ~3%, and CA universal TK is removing roughly 100,000 four-year-olds from the private market by 2025-26 (LA 48K, SD 22K, OC 18K, Riverside 15K), concentrating CA demand in under-3s.

**Phase 8, TAM, SAM, SOM with definitions.** TAM: the annual owner-value opportunity in properties entering the management or market pipeline, defined as the incremental owner net value produced by feasible alternatives rather than the value of homes to acquire. Segment it by usable square footage and location. The childcare component is built from the licensed-capacity shortfall (settled: slots per child needing care, King 0.88, San Diego 0.74, Orange 0.67, LA 0.56, Snohomish 0.51, Riverside 0.39) translated into needed facilities times achievable owner net value, adjusted for the phase-7 demand trajectory. SAM: the slice reachable by GPS's owner-management, conversion, and leasehold channels, gated by the count of properties matching the control box (C1 anchor: ~55% of small-owner stock is rate-locked per prior research) and the supply of licensable providers, whichever binds first, and say which binds; expect providers to bind in Washington (12-16 capacity needs 2-3+ year experienced licensees). SOM: the output of the phase-11 operating model only, reported as a share of SAM and flagged above 25 percent. Size the provider pipeline by county from licensing issuance data.

**Phase 9, competition and incumbent response.** The census is settled; update status only: Mission Driven Finance CARE fund is the only property-side player (25 properties, ~488 seats, San Diego / Las Vegas / Colorado, no Washington presence, philanthropy-capitalized); Wonderschool, Upwards, Winnie, BridgeCare are software-only; Fortec/Equiturn and net-lease capital do commercial centers; EPR REIT is exiting education; WA Early Learning Facilities grants are 5x oversubscribed. SB 234 and WA law force every ordinary landlord to tolerate family child care, making every landlord a latent competitor at zero premium: model the price-compression scenario where tolerant landlords cap the premium at 10 percent and state what survives. State Green Property Solutions' structural advantage if one exists (conversion capital, licensing navigation, provider pipeline, licensable-ready speed) and if none exists, say none exists.

**Phase 10, demand validation plan with decision rules.** Two assumptions gate everything: the rent premium a provider will pay, and provider supply. Design the cheapest tests: list one converted or convert-ready owner property and measure provider applications and accepted rent within N weeks; run a provider-intent campaign through county resource-and-referral agencies and family child care associations with a target of X letters of intent at the modeled rent; for California, the city-level tuition survey (Newport Beach, Irvine, Costa Mesa, and the Riverside target cities) is itself a gating validation and precedes any CA commitment. Attach decision rules of the form: fewer than X applications at the modeled premium within N weeks disproves the central case and stops owner commitments and conversion spend. Identify the exogenous triggers that move demand discontinuously (TK expansion beyond current schedule, CA RMR unfreeze, WA family-home move to the 75th percentile in July 2027, Working Connections eligibility shifts, Head Start disruption) and model each as a scenario branch with its leading indicator.

**Phase 11, phased operating model with debt at current rates.** Monthly engine over 60 months across the six counties with entry gates, per-property pipeline stages (control, convert, license dead months, place provider, stabilize), conversion capex drawn at searched current rates where debt-funded, provider-turnover events with re-placement vacancy, working capital including deposits and rent-commencement lag, property management fee revenue on both the A book and B book, staffing steps for Green Property Solutions at loaded wages, insurance, and the funnel cost per placed provider. Include at least one channel scenario beyond one-by-one placement: a master agreement with a provider network, resource-and-referral agency, or employer-sponsored childcare buyer that delivers multiple placements per agreement. Include rent architecture tested against provider viability: flat premium versus base-plus-percentage versus capex-amortization rider. Output cash trough, month, equity required with an operating floor, DSCR at month 60, and capital to terminal EBITDA, judged against phase 0.

**Phase 12, risk quantification.** Probabilities across the scenario space and expected value, ruin probability against committed equity, tornado ranked by effect on 60-month cash including the two-way interaction of rent premium crossed with provider-turnover rate. Tail risks with cites: licensing revocation or abuse allegation at a property and its vacancy and reputational consequences, statute amendment in either direction, insurance market withdrawal from childcare-use dwellings, and the landlord's liability exposure for a tenant-operated facility including what standard landlord policies exclude.

**Phase 13, terminal value and exit, then verification.** Value the month-60 management and service platform, not a property portfolio: recurring owner-management revenue, conversion coordination, provider-placement economics, and, if selected, center operating-company economics. Owners retain the real-estate terminal value. Verification is mandatory: compute the full model in an independent scripted calculation first, print reference values, build the workbook second, recalculate headlessly to zero formula errors, and tie every NPV, corridor, and verdict cell to the reference to the dollar, printing the comparison. Known failure modes: sheet names containing hyphens must be single-quoted in cross-sheet formulas; never place an input in a row a later header write will overwrite; when adding rows, re-audit every hardcoded cell anchor referencing that sheet.
