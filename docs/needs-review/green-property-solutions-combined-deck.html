The Conversion Product, Under Two Pricing Lenses, Across Six Counties

Green Property Solutions converts other owners' single-family and small residential properties to licensed family child care use. The owner keeps the property and earns premium rent. A licensed resident provider operates the care business. Green Property Solutions handles the conversion, provider placement, and ongoing services for fees. The owner is the customer; the decision metric is dollars of productive rent per square foot per year.

This single file combines the two pricing questions that decide the analysis, and the supporting work behind both. Lens A prices care at what providers actually charge in each county today. Lens B prices care at a fixed host card of $2,000 per month for a child who is not potty-trained and $1,500 for one who is, with a capacity ramp. The two lenses disagree about California, and that disagreement is the whole decision.

Counties in decision order: Orange, San Diego, Riverside, Los Angeles, then Snohomish, King. Every figure recomputed from the Python reference models (v2 market-pricing, v3 host-card) before this file was written; time-sensitive inputs searched July 2026 and re-checked against primary sources (29 of 30 claims confirmed, 1 corrected).

The answerBoth lenses at once

Washington Sells Under Any Pricing. California Is a Bet on One Number.

Washington is the launch market, and it is robust. Under market pricing (Lens A) a King or Snohomish provider can pay 2.4 to 2.5 times market rent; the owner's productivity roughly doubles and the conversion pays back inside 8 months. Under the lower host card (Lens B) Washington stays clearly positive. It wins whether care is priced high or low.

California has no product at today's tuition (Lens A), and only a conditional one at the host card (Lens B). At prevailing rates a provider in San Diego, Riverside, or Los Angeles cannot outbid the family tenant already in the home. The $2,000 / $1,500 card reopens all four counties on paper, Riverside best of all, but only if that card actually collects, and it sits 25 to 60 percent above prevailing California family-child-care rates.

So the California go or no-go reduces to one measurement: can a provider in the target ZIPs collect roughly $1,500 to $2,000 per child per month? Two weeks of primary tuition survey settles it. Nothing else in the California case moves the verdict as much.

How to read thisThe two lenses defined

The Two Lenses, and Why They Disagree

Lens A · Market pricing

Care is priced at what family child care actually charges in each county now: California county medians (2019 to 2022 vintage) escalated 30 percent to a 2024 anchor; Washington at prior-research rates of $2,300 to $2,600 per infant. A full 12-child license at 85 percent fill. This is the conservative, evidence-anchored lens.

Reference model: green_property_solutions_model_v2.py.

Lens B · Host price card

Care is priced at a fixed card you set: $2,000 per month for a child who is not potty-trained (capped at 4 such seats per the infant limit), $1,500 for a potty-trained child. Capacity ramps from X children with one provider, for Y months, to Z children once an assistant is added. Defaults X=6, Y=12, Z=12.

Reference model: green_property_solutions_model_v3.py.

They disagree because the card is uniform across counties while market tuition is not. Under Lens A, California tuition is pinned near the state subsidy ceiling, so the provider's ceiling falls below market rent. Under Lens B, the same $1,667 blended price is charged everywhere, so the only thing that varies is the rent the provider must beat, which inverts the county ranking. The rest of this file works each lens, then reconciles them.

Lens A · Care priced at market

MechanismThe number that decides each county

The Provider Affordability Ceiling vs Market Rent

The rent a provider can pay is derived, not assumed: gross tuition for 10.2 children (12-child license at 85 percent fill), minus a market-wage assistant, supplies, insurance, a 10 percent Green Property Solutions services fee, and a $55,000 living income for herself, with the result capped at 35 percent of gross revenue. Where that ceiling clears market rent, the owner has a reason to convert. Where it does not, a family tenant outbids the provider.

Lens B · Care priced at your host card

The card and the rampWhat changes

The $2,000 / $1,500 Card, on an X to Z Capacity Ramp

The price card

  • $2,000 per month, child not potty-trained, capped at 4 such seats (infant limit).
  • $1,500 per month, child potty-trained.
  • Revenue-optimal fill takes the four $2,000 seats first, then $1,500 seats to capacity, blending to about $1,667 per child at a full 12-child home.

The capacity ramp

  • X children with one provider (no assistant) for the first Y months, the single-provider license phase.
  • Z children once an assistant is added after Y. Defaults X=6, Y=12 months, Z=12.
  • Four licensing dead months precede revenue.

Because the card is the same in every county, it lifts California off the subsidy-pinned floor that sank it under Lens A. The blended $1,667 clears the $1,394 to $1,515 crossover bar everywhere. That is why the next slide shows all six counties positive at full ramp, and why the story quietly moves from "which county has pricing power" to "which county has the lowest rent to beat."

Lens B · Care priced at your host card

The catchRead before trusting the ranking

Two Things the Card Does That You Have to See

  • It reopens California on a number that is 25 to 60 percent above prevailing rates. Riverside market family-child-care runs near $1,300 per child; the card charges $1,500 to $2,000. So Riverside's first-place NPV is also the most exposed: best economics, thinnest household incomes, worst tuition-to-income ratio (0.27). The California result is real only if the card collects, and Riverside carries the most collection risk of the four.
  • It underprices Washington. Washington market tuition is $2,300 to $2,600, above the card, so applying the card there cuts the Washington premium from roughly $4,500 to $5,000 (Lens A) down to $1,700 to $2,300 (Lens B). In Washington the card should be a floor, not the price. The optimized Washington case keeps market pricing and the higher premium.

Lens B · Care priced at your host card

The rampWhere the value leaks

The Solo Phase Is a Cost. License at Eight and Compress It.

At the default six-child solo phase the provider cannot cover market rent in five of six counties (premium −$1,016 in Orange to +$88 in Riverside), so every month spent at X=6 before the assistant arrives burns owner premium. On the King baseline the owner NPV falls about $1,800 for each extra month of the solo phase. Two levers fix it: license at eight children (a California small home permits eight with the required age mix), which turns the solo phase positive at +$340 per month, and compress Y with the state director-experience waiver where it can be granted.

What to doSequence that follows from the numbers

The Plan Both Lenses Support

  • Launch in Washington now, priced at market, not at the card. King and Snohomish clear every test under both lenses; pricing at market keeps the full $4,500-plus premium. First conversion in a 98012-class ZIP. The year-one milestone is one signed provider-owner pair, because provider recruiting, not demand, is the binding Washington constraint.
  • Design the ramp to X=8 with the shortest legal solo phase. The solo phase destroys value at X=6; licensing at eight and compressing Y with the director-experience waiver is worth tens of thousands of NPV per door.
  • Do not enter California on the model. Enter on a two-week tuition survey. Survey Newport Beach, Irvine, and Costa Mesa (Orange) and the Riverside target ZIPs for actual collectible rates. Above about $1,515 per child, Orange opens under both lenses; the card's California NPVs become real only where the survey confirms $1,500-plus collects. Riverside, the paper winner, needs the most proof.
  • Treat the host card as a floor, not a ceiling. Where market tuition exceeds the card (all of Washington, possibly affluent Orange ZIPs), price to market and pocket the difference; the card protects the downside, it should not cap the upside.

Supporting analysis

AssumptionsWhat each verdict rests on

The Load-Bearing Assumptions

  • The California verdict rests entirely on tuition. Under Lens A it is the escalated 2019 to 2022 county medians; under Lens B it is whether the $2,000 / $1,500 card collects at 25 to 60 percent above those medians. This one input, in both directions, is the single largest uncertainty and the cheapest to resolve with primary work.
  • The Washington verdict rests on provider supply, not demand. Premiums of $4,500-plus require providers willing and licensed at 12-child capacity. The recruiting pipeline (DCYF Welcome Team, Wonderschool, training cohorts) is the mechanism; it is a recruiting bet, not a market bet.
  • The two-principal operating capacity is unproven. Prior research prices operator time at $125 per hour and flags 150 to 250 door capacity for two principals as the central unvalidated assumption; the platform ramp above assumes it holds.
  • The provider-continuity haircut (8 percent) is a proxy from licensing churn data (12 to 14 percent annual closures). The structural floor caps the damage: a departed provider returns the property to market-tenant rent, the rent it earns today.
  • Option C, assisted care, is outlined not modeled. Six-bed board-and-care grosses $4,000 to $6,500 per resident per month but carries 24/7 staffing, an 80-hour administrator certification, a 4 to 18 month licensing timeline, and mandatory $1M/$3M liability. The same affordability-ceiling test applies with an RCFE operator; inputs are staged, the run is pending.
